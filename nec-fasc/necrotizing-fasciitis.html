Necrotizing Fasciitis on CT

This is a surgical, not radiological, diagnosis — imaging must never delay debridement. Copy-ready findings/impressions — replace [bracketed] text.

🚨 Emergency surgical debridement; call the surgeon now. Do not wait for CT if clinical suspicion is high. Absent gas on CT does not exclude necrotizing fasciitis — streptococcal NF frequently has no gas.

Pathophysiology & Classification

Background & types

💡PearlType I vs Type II
  • Type I (polymicrobial, ~80%): Mixed aerobic/anaerobic organisms (e.g., E. coli, Klebsiella, anaerobes). Soft-tissue gas is a recognised feature of this gas-forming type. Classically in older, immunocompromised, or diabetic patients. Most common in the trunk/perineum.
  • Type II (monomicrobial, ~20%): Group A Streptococcus ± S. aureus. Younger patients, often no predisposing wound. Gas is characteristically absent — never exclude on this basis.
  • Type III (monomicrobial Gram-negative, e.g. Vibrio vulnificus, Aeromonas): Seawater exposure or seafood injury; extremely rapid. (Clostridial gas gangrene / fungal disease is often classed separately as Type IV.)
  • Fournier gangrene: NF of the perineum/genitalia — a distinct clinical entity, same CT principles apply.

CT Findings — Positive Signs

Gas along the deep fascial plane (most specific)

🔍FindingFascial gas — the most specific CT sign
  • Linear or bubbly foci of gas tracking along the deep fascial plane, deep to the subcutaneous fat, superficial to muscle or within intermuscular fascial planes.
  • Key distinction: gas is at or below the level of deep fascia, not confined to the subcutaneous layer or skin surface (which may be seen in simple cellulitis with crepitus).
  • Gas may extend along fascial planes far beyond the clinically apparent margins — trace it in all planes (MPR coronal/sagittal essential).
  • Sensitivity ~55%, specificity ~97% for NF — absence of gas does not exclude.

Fascial fluid & thickening

📏ThresholdDeep fascial fluid > 3 mm
  • Fluid tracking along the deep fascial plane > 3 mm thick is the most sensitive CT sign (~80%), present even when gas is absent.
  • Fascial thickening and edema without frank gas — look for loss of the normal sharp fascial interface.
  • Both signs together (fluid + thickening) give higher specificity than either alone.
🔍FindingNon-enhancement of fascia & muscle
  • On contrast CT: absence of enhancement of the fascia and/or adjacent muscle indicates devascularization — highly specific for deep infection with tissue necrosis.
  • Compare with the contralateral side for asymmetric muscle enhancement.
  • Rim-enhancing fluid collections within intermuscular compartments.
  • Subcutaneous fat thickening and infiltration extending beyond the apparent clinical margins.

Extent & compartment spread

🔍FindingMapping the extent of disease
  • Trace fascial gas and fluid in axial + coronal + sagittal MPR — disease spreads far beyond the skin wound.
  • Document involvement of specific fascial compartments (e.g., Colles fascia → Scarpa fascia → anterior abdominal wall in Fournier gangrene).
  • Note proximity to critical structures: retroperitoneum, psoas, pelvis, perirectal space, scrotum/labia, thigh compartments.
  • Assess for associated abscess, septic thrombophlebitis, or osteomyelitis of adjacent bone.

Fournier Gangrene

Perineal variant

🚨CritFournier gangrene — perineal NF
  • NF of the scrotum/penis, labia, or perineum — high mortality (15–40%) if not debrided immediately.
  • CT: gas tracking along the perineal/scrotal fasciae (dartos, Buck, Colles), often extending up the anterior abdominal wall via Scarpa fascia.
  • May originate from anorectal, urogenital, or skin sources — look for the inciting cause (fistula, abscess, urethral stricture, colorectal perforation).
  • Map the cranial extent along Scarpa fascia — surgical team must know how high it reaches.
  • Non-enhancement of scrotal wall/perineal structures; thickened, edematous fascia; perineal fluid.

LRINEC Score Context

Lab-Radiology Integration

🧪ProtocolLRINEC score — use with caution

The Laboratory Risk Indicator for Necrotizing Fasciitis (LRINEC) is a laboratory score (not an imaging finding): CRP, WBC, Hgb, Na, Cr, and glucose. Score ≥ 6 is considered high risk; score ≥ 8 is very high risk.

  • LRINEC was derived retrospectively and has limited sensitivity (~60–80%) in prospective validation — a low score does not exclude NF.
  • CT adds anatomical localisation and extent — but neither CT nor LRINEC should delay surgical exploration when clinical suspicion is high.
  • The "finger test" (blunt dissection in the OR, no resistance along fascial planes, necrotic tissue) remains the diagnostic gold standard.

Key Mimic — Cellulitis & Abscess

Differentiating NF from cellulitis

⚠️PitfallSimple cellulitis / abscess vs NF
  • Cellulitis/abscess: Enhancement of superficial tissues is preserved; fat stranding is superficial and respects fascial boundaries; no deep fascial gas or fluid; rim-enhancing abscess may be present but is isolated.
  • NF red flags on CT: Deep fascial gas > any air more than isolated skin; fluid > 3 mm tracking along fascia; loss of fascial plane definition; non-enhancing fascia or muscle; spread beyond one compartment.
  • Equivocal CT does not exclude NF — communicate uncertainty explicitly. "CT does not exclude deep fascial involvement; surgical exploration is the definitive test."
  • Gas in the superficial subcutaneous tissue alone (e.g., gas-forming abscess, recent wound, decubitus ulcer) is not diagnostic of NF — location relative to deep fascia is the key discriminator.
💡PearlCT sensitivity & the "do not exclude" statement
  • CT sensitivity for NF: ~88–93% (specificity ~93–97%) in most series, but varies by gas presence.
  • A normal CT does not exclude NF — especially Type II streptococcal disease early in its course. If clinical suspicion remains high after a negative CT, the patient needs OR exploration, not observation.
  • Always include this language in equivocal reports: "CT does not exclude necrotizing fasciitis; correlation with clinical findings and surgical consultation is required."

CT Technique

Optimal protocol

🧪ProtocolCT technique for soft-tissue infection
  • IV contrast (portal-venous phase) is essential for assessing fascial/muscle enhancement and devascularization. Unenhanced CT can show gas but cannot assess viability.
  • Wide field of view covering the full extent of the clinical region — for perineal disease include abdomen to mid-thigh at minimum (map Scarpa fascia ascent).
  • Reconstruct with soft-tissue and bone windows; soft-tissue windows best for gas; bone windows confirm calcific vs gas density (HU < −100 = gas).
  • Coronal and sagittal MPR mandatory — axial alone misses cranio-caudal fascial spread.
  • Oral contrast generally not required; adds delay.

Search pattern

  • 1. Identify the clinical zone on the scout/axial — set your field broadly, including one fascial layer above and below the clinically marked region.
  • 2. Toggle bone windows (HU < −100): look for gas at the deep fascial interface and within intermuscular fasciae — trace cranially and caudally on coronal MPR.
  • 3. Soft-tissue windows with IV contrast: assess enhancement of the deep fascia and underlying muscle — compare both sides.
  • 4. Measure depth of fluid collections along the fascial plane (> 3 mm is significant).
  • 5. For perineal disease: follow Colles → Scarpa → anterior abdominal wall; look for an inciting source (anorectal, urological).
  • 6. Map the cranio-caudal extent of gas/fluid for surgical planning — include which compartments are involved.
  • 7. Document any adjacent abscess, septic thrombosis, osteomyelitis, or retroperitoneal extension.
  • 8. If gas is absent but fluid/non-enhancement present: explicitly state CT does not exclude NF and recommend surgical consultation.
