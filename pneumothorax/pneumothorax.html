Pneumothorax and Tension on CT

Pleural air — simple, occult, tension, hydropneumothorax, traumatic, and iatrogenic. Copy-ready findings and impressions — replace [bracketed] text. A workstation aid, not a substitute for local protocol or clinical judgment.

🚨 Tension pneumothorax is a clinical diagnosis — decompress immediately; do not wait for CT confirmation. If the ventilated or injured patient deteriorates and CT shows mediastinal shift, flattened IVC, diaphragm inversion, and a massively enlarged ipsilateral pleural space — call the team now. Needle decompression or finger thoracostomy first; imaging second.

Orientation & Protocol

Protocol — how to acquire and window

🧪ProtocolChest CT in the trauma or dyspnoeic patient
  • Standard trauma CT chest (non-contrast or portal-venous phase in the polytrauma run) is more than adequate — you do not need a dedicated pulmonary PE protocol to detect pneumothorax.
  • Lung windows (W ≈ 1500, L ≈ −500) for pleural air, pulmonary contusion, bulla. Switch to mediastinal windows (W ≈ 400, L ≈ 40) to assess mediastinal shift, IVC calibre, and cardiac displacement.
  • For a supine patient (most trauma and ventilated patients), air collects anteriorly and non-dependently — do not expect the lateral apical collection seen on an upright CXR.
  • Always scroll through the full chest in lung windows — small anterior pneumothoraces are easily missed on a single representative image.
  • Reformats: coronal helps quantify volume and shows diaphragm depression; sagittal shows the anterior pleural space extent.

Detection — Finding Pleural Air

The visceral pleural line

🔍How to find itThe visceral pleural line on CT

The definitive CT sign is a thin white line (visceral pleura) separated from the chest wall by a lucent (air-density, ≈ −900 HU) space devoid of lung markings. On lung windows:

  • The visceral pleural line is often only 1–2 mm thick but sharply defined — brighter than the surrounding compressed lung.
  • No bronchovascular markings should be visible peripheral to the line.
  • In the supine position, the anterior and medial pleural recesses fill first — check the retrosternal and anteromedial spaces carefully on every axial slice before scrolling past.
  • On soft-tissue windows, the air pocket is pitch black against the liver/spleen — useful for confirming a right-sided anterior PTX adjacent to the liver.

Occult and anterior pneumothorax

An occult pneumothorax is one detected on CT but missed on a preceding supine anteroposterior chest radiograph — present in up to 15–20% of blunt trauma patients. It is anterior and non-dependent, overlying the heart and anterior chest wall on supine CXR, where it is invisible on a single projection.

💡PearlDeep sulcus sign on CXR — the tip-off to look harder on CT

On a supine CXR, the deep sulcus sign is a sharply demarcated, unusually lucent and deep costophrenic angle, often with a clearly visible lateral diaphragm margin. It is the radiographic correlate of anterior pleural air displacing the lung medially and superiorly. If you see it on the referring image, look even harder on CT anteriorly and confirm size.

⚠️PitfallMistaking a bulla for a pneumothorax
  • An emphysematous bulla can look identical on a single slice — both are air-density, both can be large.
  • Key distinctions: a bulla has a thin but perceptible wall (typically curvilinear, slightly hyperdense); it is contained within the lung parenchyma with surrounding vessels compressed around it; the visceral pleural line is at the outer bulla wall, not displaced inward.
  • Scroll through adjacent slices in multiple planes — a PTX forms a continuous pleural crescent around the lung margin; a bulla stays focal and parenchymal.
  • When in doubt, look for lung sliding on ultrasound or review the coronal CT — a large PTX will show the collapsed lung edge hanging freely away from the lateral chest wall, whereas a bulla preserves normal pleural apposition elsewhere.

Size and volume estimation

📏MeasureQuantifying pneumothorax on CT

CT volumetric assessment is more accurate than the Light criteria (used for CXR). Practical approaches:

  • Volumetric segmentation: available on most PACS — segment pleural air and report volume in mL. Guides drain vs conservative management.
  • Average interpleural distance: measure the maximum distance between the collapsed lung edge and the chest wall at the apex, mid-chest, and base on the coronal reformat; an interpleural distance >2 cm generally indicates a large pneumothorax (per BTS sizing).
  • Rim width: a simple axial measurement at the widest point — report as "rim of [X] mm of pleural air in the [anterior / lateral / posterior] pleural space."
  • Always report whether the lung appears fully expandable (smooth collapsed edge) or if there is underlying parenchymal disease (consolidation, contusion, atelectasis) that may prevent re-expansion.

Tension Physiology — The Killer

CT features of tension pneumothorax

Tension physiology develops when air enters the pleural space and cannot escape — pressure builds until it compresses mediastinal structures. This is a clinical emergency managed on presentation; CT findings are confirmatory and seen in patients who reach the scanner alive (ventilated patients especially).

🚨CriticalVentilated patients: do not wait for mediastinal shift

Positive-pressure ventilation can maintain near-normal hemodynamics even with a significant PTX — until decompensation is abrupt and lethal. In ventilated trauma patients, any new anterior pleural air ≥1 cm or any enlarging PTX is typically drained, regardless of whether mediastinal shift has developed. Discuss with the team immediately.

Specific Entities

Hydropneumothorax and hemopneumothorax

Air–fluid or air–blood interfaces in the pleural space create a sharp air–fluid level on erect or semi-erect positioning. On CT the combination is unmistakable — lucent air non-dependently and dependent hyperdense fluid (blood ≈ 35–70 HU, serous fluid ≈ 0–20 HU).

🔍How to find itHemopneumothorax in trauma
  • Report both components: volume of pleural air (rim width, extent) and estimated volume of pleural fluid (measure largest AP depth of dependent collection on axial; a rough estimate only: ~1 cm of dependent fluid depth on axial CT ≈ ~100–150 mL per side).
  • Look for active extravasation — a focal hyperdense blush (≥10 HU above adjacent hematoma) in the arterial phase, enlarging on delayed — prompts urgent intervention.
  • Check the adjacent ribs, intercostal vessels, pulmonary hilar vessels, and the aorta / subclavian arteries as sources.
  • A tension hemothorax can occur — same CT features as tension PTX but with a dense fluid-filled pleural space instead of (or alongside) air.

Iatrogenic, post-biopsy, and post-procedure PTX

The most common causes are CT-guided lung biopsy, thoracentesis, central venous catheter insertion (especially subclavian), and positive-pressure ventilation (barotrauma). Post-biopsy PTX appears immediately or within 1–4 hours.

💡PearlPost-biopsy PTX — when to call it significant

After CT-guided biopsy, any PTX <1 cm anterior rim on the immediate post-biopsy scan is observed with repeat CXR in 2–4 hours. PTX >2 cm, symptomatic, or enlarging on follow-up usually requires aspiration or drainage. Document the initial size precisely on the biopsy report so that the follow-up CXR can be compared numerically.

⚠️PitfallCatamenial pneumothorax — frequently missed

Right-sided PTX in a premenopausal woman with recurrent episodes should raise this diagnosis even on the first CT. Look carefully at the right hemidiaphragm for small defects (fenestrations) and nodular pleural/diaphragmatic implants. Standard lung windows can underestimate pleural nodules — review on soft-tissue windows as well. Always note the timing relative to the menstrual cycle in the clinical correlation.

Trauma don't-miss checklist

🚨CriticalTrauma chest CT — scan completely before calling "no PTX"
  • Anterior pleural space (retrosternal, parasternal) — the first place to look in a supine scan.
  • Apical cap — small apical collections are real PTX, not just skin fold artifact.
  • Skin folds are a common false-positive pitfall on CXR but on CT are easy to dismiss — they extend outside the thoracic cavity and have no collapsed-lung edge medially.
  • Bilateral PTX — a left-side focus should not distract from a smaller right-side one.
  • Subcutaneous emphysema does not equal PTX but its presence on CT should prompt a thorough search of both pleural spaces — it often accompanies a small anterior PTX.
  • Post-intubation: check position of endotracheal tube, confirm correct depth, and assess for right main-stem intubation causing left-sided atelectasis (vs PTX).

⚠️PitfallGiant bulla + PTX can coexist

A patient with bullous emphysema can develop a PTX in the remaining pleural space alongside the bulla. Look for a visible visceral pleural line displaced away from the chest wall beyond the bulla wall, and a new air space that does not match the prior bulla morphology. Comparison with prior imaging is invaluable.

Search pattern

  1. Lung windows first, anterior pleural space — on every axial slice from apex to diaphragm, check the anteromedial and retrosternal recess before moving on.
  2. Trace any lucent crescent outward from the lung surface — confirm there are no bronchovascular markings in the lucency.
  3. Measure the maximum rim width and note the extent (apex to base, anterior to posterior).
  4. Assess mediastinum on mediastinal windows — tracheal position at the thoracic inlet, cardiac displacement, IVC calibre at the diaphragm.
  5. Both hemidiaphragms — depression or inversion on the ipsilateral side.
  6. Ipsilateral rib cage expansion — axial comparison of the two hemi-thoraces.
  7. Dependent pleural space for fluid (hemothorax component) — measure depth, estimate volume, check for active blush.
  8. Contralateral side — do not miss a bilateral pneumothorax; an ipsilateral finding can draw attention away from the other side.
  9. Subcutaneous emphysema — if present, re-examine ipsilateral and contralateral pleural spaces meticulously.
  10. Underlying parenchyma — bullae, emphysema, contusion, consolidation that would modify management or recurrence risk.
  11. Coronal and sagittal reformats — confirm size, show diaphragm morphology, help distinguish bulla from PTX in equivocal cases.
