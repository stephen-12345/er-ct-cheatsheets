Acute Aortic Syndrome on CT

Dissection · intramural hematoma · penetrating ulcer · ruptured aneurysm. Copy-ready findings and impressions — replace [bracketed] text. A workstation aid, not a substitute for local protocol or clinical correlation.

🚨 Stanford A (any ascending aorta involvement) = surgical emergency. Page the surgeon / call the referring clinician before you finish dictating. Always look for the three killers: pericardial blood / tamponade, aortic regurgitation, and coronary/branch malperfusion.

Orientation & Protocol

What counts as acute aortic syndrome

AAS is the umbrella for acute, life-threatening aortic wall pathology presenting with "aortic" chest/back/abdominal pain. The four overlapping entities:

EntityOne-line definitionDefining CT sign
Aortic dissection (AD)Blood enters the media through an intimal tear → true + false lumenIntimal flap separating two lumina
Intramural hematoma (IMH)Hemorrhage within the media, no intimal tear / flow in false lumenCrescentic high-attenuation wall thickening, no flap, no enhancement
Penetrating atherosclerotic ulcer (PAU)Ulcerated plaque erodes through intima into mediaContrast-filled outpouching beyond the lumen + adjacent IMH
Contained / frank ruptureAneurysm or any AAS lesion breaches the adventitiaPeriaortic/mediastinal/retroperitoneal hematoma, active extravasation
🧪ProtocolGet the scan right or you will miss it
  • Non-contrast first, then ECG-gated CT angiography of the chest (extend through abdomen/pelvis to the femorals when dissection is suspected, to map extent and access).
  • The non-contrast series is mandatory: IMH and acute thrombus are hyperdense (~60–70 HU) against the wall and become invisible once contrast is in the lumen.
  • ECG gating abolishes aortic-root pulsation artifact — the #1 cause of a false "ascending dissection."
  • Arterial-phase timing on the aorta; add a delayed phase if you suspect slow false-lumen filling, endoleak, or active bleeding.
  • Triple rule-out (coronaries + aorta + PE) only when pretest probability fits; it compromises any single bed.
⚠️PitfallClassic mimics of an ascending "flap"
  • Pulsation / motion artifact at the aortic root (≈ 9–3 o'clock or right anterior / left posterior wall) — straight line, changes between cardiac phases, crosses the wall. Use the gated series.
  • Left brachiocephalic vein, superior pericardial recess, periaortic atelectasis/effusion abutting the aorta.
  • Streak / beam-hardening from dense contrast in the SVC or a pacer lead.
  • Confirm any flap on ≥2 planes and on the gated phase before you call dissection.

💡PearlThe arch-only gray zone

An arch dissection not involving the ascending aorta is sometimes called "non-A non-B." Describe the proximal extent explicitly (e.g., "begins at the proximal arch between innominate and left carotid") so the surgeon can classify it.

Core dissection findings

🔍How to find itThe intimal flap

A thin linear filling defect within the contrast-opacified lumen, dividing it into true and false lumina. Trace it on axial and a reformat (sagittal-oblique candy-cane) from the root to the iliacs.

  • Define the proximal and distal extent, the entry tear (intimal interruption), and any re-entry tears.
  • Spiraling flap is typical — the true lumen often rotates anteriorly in the arch and lies anteromedial in the descending aorta.

True vs false lumen — get it right (TEVAR depends on it)

FeatureTrue lumenFalse lumen
SizeUsually smallerUsually larger, surrounds the true lumen
WallBounded by calcified intimaBounded by outer aortic wall (no calcification)
Beak sign—Acute wedge angle of hematoma at the flap margin — specific for false lumen
Cobweb sign—Thin residual media strands crossing the lumen — only in false lumen
OpacificationBrisk, equal to non-dissected aortaOften delayed / slower; may partially thrombose
ContinuityContinuous with non-dissected aorta & coronary/arch originsSurrounds true lumen, often outer curvature
⚠️PitfallDon't mistake a thrombosed false lumen for IMH

A completely thrombosed false lumen can look crescentic like IMH. Look for intimal calcium displaced inward, a residual flap, and a beak. IMH has a smooth inner margin and no flap; a thrombosed false lumen usually has an irregular interface and may show a small re-entry channel.

Malperfusion & complications — the part that changes the call

For every dissection, walk the branch vessels and report each as perfused from true lumen / false lumen / both, and whether it is compromised (static or dynamic obstruction):

  • Coronary ostia (esp. RCA) → myocardial ischemia. Look for hemopericardium.
  • Arch vessels (innominate, L carotid, L subclavian) → stroke, limb ischemia.
  • Celiac / SMA → bowel ischemia. Renal arteries → renal infarct / AKI.
  • Iliacs / femorals → leg ischemia; also dictates endovascular access.
🚨Call nowHigh-risk / rupture features
  • Hemopericardium / tamponade (high-attenuation pericardial fluid) — type A until proven otherwise.
  • Hemomediastinum, hemothorax (usually left), periaortic hematoma.
  • Active contrast extravasation beyond the aortic wall.
  • Aortic regurgitation physiology / dilated root, flap prolapse across the valve.
  • Rapidly increasing aortic diameter or false-lumen aneurysm > 6 cm.

Intramural Hematoma (IMH)

IMH — what it looks like

🔍How to find itCrescentic high-density wall, best on non-contrast

Smooth, circumferential or crescentic thickening of the aortic wall > 5 mm, hyperdense (~60–70 HU) on the non-contrast series, with no intimal flap and no enhancing false lumen after contrast.

  • Internal displacement of intimal calcium is the give-away that the high density is in the wall, not lining it.
  • Does not spiral; maintains a constant circumferential relationship along its length (unlike a dissection flap).
📏ThresholdIMH high-risk features
  • Maximal wall thickness ≥ 11 mm and aortic diameter ≥ 48–50 mm predict progression.
  • Ascending (type A) IMH is managed like type A dissection — surgical.
  • Watch for evolution to overt dissection, focal contrast outpouching ("ulcer-like projection"), or pericardial/pleural effusion.

Penetrating Atherosclerotic Ulcer (PAU)

PAU — what it looks like

🔍How to find itContrast crater beyond the intima in a diseased aorta

A focal, contrast-filled outpouching projecting beyond the expected aortic lumen through a region of heavy atherosclerotic plaque, typically in the descending thoracic aorta, usually with adjacent intramural hematoma.

  • Distinguish from a simple atherosclerotic ulcer (does not breach the intima, no IMH) and from a saccular aneurysm.
  • High-risk: increasing depth/width, associated IMH, periaortic stranding/hematoma, or symptoms.

Ruptured / Leaking Aortic Aneurysm

Ruptured AAA & impending-rupture signs

🚨Call nowFrank rupture
  • Retroperitoneal / periaortic hematoma adjacent to an aneurysm (most ruptures are into the left retroperitoneum).
  • Active contrast extravasation into the hematoma on arterial/delayed phase.
  • Loss of the normal fat plane between aorta and adjacent structures.
⚠️PitfallImpending rupture — call it before it bleeds
  • Hyperattenuating crescent sign: high-density crescent in the mural thrombus/wall (acute intramural hemorrhage) — a herald of rupture.
  • Draped aorta sign: posterior aortic wall drapes over a vertebral body, no fat plane.
  • Focal discontinuity of intimal calcification; rapid diameter increase; large size (> 5.5 cm).

Reporting checklist

✅ChecklistDon't sign off without
  • Type (A vs B / DeBakey) and proximal + distal extent.
  • Entry tear location; primary fenestration.
  • True vs false lumen identification + which feeds each major branch.
  • Branch / malperfusion: coronary, arch vessels, celiac, SMA, renals, iliacs.
  • Complications: pericardial blood/tamponade, AR, hemothorax, mediastinal/retroperitoneal hematoma, active extravasation, end-organ infarct.
  • Maximal aortic diameter + level; for TEVAR planning: proximal/distal landing zones, access vessels.

Search pattern

  • 1. Review the non-contrast series for high-attenuation crescent (IMH), displaced intimal calcium, and pericardial/pleural blood before looking at the angiogram.
  • 2. Trace the aorta on the gated arterial series from root → arch → descending → abdominal → iliacs on axial + centerline reformat.
  • 3. Identify any flap; define entry tear, extent, and true vs false lumen (beak, cobweb, calcium).
  • 4. Walk every branch vessel: coronaries, arch vessels, celiac, SMA, renals, iliacs — note which lumen supplies each and any obstruction.
  • 5. Hunt for complications: hemopericardium/tamponade, AR, hemomediastinum/hemothorax, retroperitoneal hematoma, active extravasation, end-organ infarct (kidney, bowel, spinal cord, limb).
  • 6. Measure maximal diameters perpendicular to the centerline; describe landing zones for TEVAR if relevant.
  • 7. If type A or any rupture sign → communicate immediately and document the conversation.
