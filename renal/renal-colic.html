Renal Colic / Ureterolithiasis on CT

Non-contrast stone protocol: find the stone, size it, locate it, and call infection. Replace [bracketed] text.

⚠️ Two things change everything: stone size + location (predicts spontaneous passage) and obstructing stone + infection (perinephric stranding + fever = possible pyonephrosis, a urologic emergency needing decompression).

Search pattern

  • 1. Trace both ureters end-to-end on bone windows; check the three narrowings.
  • 2. Stone: size, side, location, HU; rim sign vs phlebolith.
  • 3. Obstruction: hydroureteronephrosis, perinephric stranding.
  • 4. Infection signs → pyonephrosis (communicate).
  • 5. If no stone, look hard for an alternative cause of pain.

Protocol & Detection

Non-contrast stone CT

🧪ProtocolLow-dose non-contrast
  • Non-contrast CT abdomen/pelvis is the standard; nearly all stones are radiodense on CT (uric acid stones are lucent on radiograph but visible on CT).
  • Exception: indinavir / pure matrix stones may be invisible.
  • Measure on bone/wide window to avoid overestimating size from blooming.
🔍How to find itTrace each ureter top to bottom
  • Follow each ureter from the renal pelvis → over the iliac vessels → to the UVJ. Stones lodge at the 3 narrow points: UPJ, pelvic brim/iliac crossing, and UVJ (most common).
  • Soft-tissue rim sign (edematous ureteral wall around the stone) helps distinguish a ureteral stone from a phlebolith.
  • Phleboliths are rounded with a central lucency and often have a "comet tail" — not in the ureteral course.

Secondary & Severity

Obstruction & size

📏ThresholdSize predicts passage
  • ≤ 5 mm: likely to pass spontaneously. 5–10 mm: variable. > 10 mm: unlikely; often needs intervention.
  • Report size (mm), location, side, and density (HU) — high HU stones resist lithotripsy.
  • Secondary signs of obstruction: hydroureteronephrosis, perinephric/periureteric fat stranding, asymmetric renal enlargement, delayed nephrogram (if contrast given).
🚨Call nowInfected obstructed system
  • Obstructing stone + perinephric stranding, urothelial thickening, layering debris/gas in the collecting system + fever → pyonephrosis / obstructive pyelonephritis.
  • This is an emergency — needs urgent decompression (stent or nephrostomy). Communicate.
⚠️PitfallDon't anchor — scan the whole study

Renal colic mimics on a non-contrast study: appendicitis, diverticulitis, leaking AAA, adnexal/ovarian pathology, bowel pathology. Look beyond the stone, especially when there is no stone to explain the pain.
