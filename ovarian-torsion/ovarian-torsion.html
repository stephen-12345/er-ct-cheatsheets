Ovarian Torsion on Imaging

US first-line with Doppler; CT often the ED finder. Replace [bracketed] text.

🚨 Ovarian torsion is a surgical emergency — time-to-OR directly determines ovarian salvage. Normal or preserved Doppler flow does NOT exclude torsion: dual ovarian blood supply can maintain flow even with complete adnexal twisting. Clinical suspicion + asymmetrically enlarged ovary = immediate gyn consult.

Anatomy & Pathophysiology

Relevant anatomy

🫀AnatomyDual blood supply — the salvage paradox
  • The ovary receives blood from the ovarian artery (branch of aorta) via the infundibulopelvic ligament and from the uterine artery via the utero-ovarian ligament.
  • Because of this dual supply, arterial inflow and venous Doppler signal can persist even after the pedicle has twisted — the classic "normal Doppler does not exclude torsion" pitfall.
  • Lymphatic and venous drainage is compromised first (lower-pressure system), causing stromal edema → ovarian enlargement. Arterial compromise follows, causing hemorrhagic infarction.
  • The right ovary twists more often than the left (sigmoid colon partially fixes the left adnexa).
  • The twisted vascular pedicle wraps around the utero-ovarian ligament, creating the whirlpool sign on imaging.
💡PearlLead-point masses predispose

Any mass that adds weight or alters the center of rotation increases torsion risk. Mature cystic teratomas (dermoids) are the most common lead point. Functional cysts > 5 cm, paraovarian cysts, and serous/mucinous cystadenomas also act as lead points. A normal-sized ovary can also torse — especially in children, where the broad ligament is more lax.

CT & US Findings

Primary CT findings

🔍FindThe characteristic CT picture
  • Asymmetrically enlarged ovary — unilateral ovarian volume > 4 cm in any dimension (many series use > 20 mL volume); contralateral ovary appears normal.
  • Peripherally displaced follicles — cortical follicles pushed outward by edematous stroma; appears as a halo of cysts at the ovarian periphery.
  • Ovarian stromal edema and heterogeneity — diffuse low-attenuation enlargement of the stroma; heterogeneous enhancement on IV contrast; may show areas of high attenuation if hemorrhagic infarction has occurred.
  • Twisted vascular pedicle / whirlpool sign — spiral or coiled appearance of the infundibulopelvic ligament vessels; best seen on axial CT with IV contrast as a beaked or swirling soft-tissue structure medial and inferior to the ovary.
  • Deviation of the uterus toward the torsed side — the pulled utero-ovarian ligament draws the uterine fundus ipsilaterally.
  • Free pelvic fluid — small to moderate; non-specific but supports the diagnosis.
  • Adnexal mass as a lead point — dermoid (fat density ± calcification), large functional cyst, cystadenoma.

Doppler US — the critical caveat

🚨CriticalNormal Doppler does NOT exclude torsion

Preserved arterial flow on color Doppler or spectral waveform is present in up to 60% of surgically confirmed torsions. The dual blood supply (ovarian artery + uterine artery arcade) allows continued perfusion despite pedicle twisting. Do not use Doppler normalcy to reassure when there is high clinical suspicion or morphologic abnormality.

  • Absent flow: strongly supports torsion; ovarian infarction likely.
  • Absent venous flow with preserved arterial flow: classic early torsion pattern — venous obstruction precedes arterial.
  • Normal arterial and venous flow: does NOT exclude torsion — requires morphologic assessment.
  • Hyperemic rim: can be seen in early torsion (reactive) or after detorsion.
🔍US FindingUS morphologic signs
  • Enlarged ovary with peripheral follicles: most reliable US morphologic sign (sensitivity ~74%).
  • Whirlpool sign on US: spiral color Doppler pattern at the twisted pedicle — high specificity when present.
  • Heterogeneous echogenic stroma: edema → diffuse low echogenicity; hemorrhage → echogenic foci.
  • Absent or diminished Doppler: helpful when present; absence does not reassure.
  • Lead-point mass: dermoid (bright with posterior shadowing), cyst, or complex mass.

Hemorrhagic infarction — late findings

⚠️PitfallLate / infarcted ovary
  • Prolonged torsion leads to hemorrhagic infarction: CT shows heterogeneous mixed-attenuation ovarian mass, peripheral rim of residual follicles, possibly high-attenuation hemorrhage (40–70 HU).
  • No enhancement of ovarian parenchyma with IV contrast = strong marker of infarction.
  • The ovary may resemble a large complex mass or mimic a tubo-ovarian abscess (TOA).
  • Even infarcted ovaries can be detorsed — histologic salvage may occur; early OR remains preferred over waiting.

Special Populations

Pediatric ovarian torsion

💡PearlChildren and adolescents
  • Most common gynecologic emergency in girls; can occur in neonates and prepubertal children.
  • Prepubertal girls often torse normal ovaries due to a longer utero-ovarian ligament with less peritoneal fixation.
  • Ovarian masses are present in ~50% of pediatric torsion (vs. ~70% in adults) — do not require a mass to diagnose.
  • Neonatal torsion often presents as an incidental calcified pelvic mass at birth (intrauterine torsion with auto-amputation).
  • Right-sided predominance maintained; pain may be intermittent with spontaneous detorsion/retorsion.
  • US is the preferred initial modality; radiation should be avoided in children when possible.

Torsion in pregnancy

💡PearlFirst and second trimester peaks
  • Most common in the first trimester (10–16 weeks), often involving a corpus luteum cyst or ovarian hyperstimulation (ART patients).
  • Second trimester: as the uterus rises, adnexal structures are lifted into the abdomen, increasing mobility and torsion risk.
  • CT is generally avoided; MRI preferred if US is non-diagnostic. In unstable patients, CT is acceptable.
  • Diagnosis and surgery are not contraindicated in pregnancy — prompt laparoscopy is standard of care.

Imaging Protocol

CT & US approach in the ED

🧪ProtocolWhen and how to image
  • US + Doppler is first-line: pelvic US (transabdominal + transvaginal) with gray-scale and color/spectral Doppler. Transvaginal improves sensitivity in obese patients.
  • CT with IV contrast is frequently the first study obtained in the ED (ordered for nonspecific pelvic pain or to exclude appendicitis/bowel pathology). Torsion is often an incidental finding on a bowel/stone CT — do not miss it.
  • CT protocol: abdomen/pelvis with IV contrast, portal venous phase; arterial or split-bolus acceptable. Oral contrast optional. Coronal and sagittal reconstructions increase conspicuity of the twisted pedicle.
  • MRI: reserved for pregnancy or equivocal US when CT is not preferred. T2 shows edematous stroma well; post-contrast T1 identifies lack of enhancement in infarction.
  • Urine βhCG in all women of reproductive age before CT — excludes ectopic pregnancy and identifies pregnancy (reduces CT if negative for urgent cases).
  • Surgical diagnosis: laparoscopy is both diagnostic and therapeutic — a normal CT or positive Doppler does not preclude operative exploration when clinical suspicion is high.
💡PearlThe CT non-contrast trap

On non-contrast CT, the twisted vascular pedicle and ovarian enhancement pattern are not assessable. Report ovarian asymmetry, peripheral follicles, and pelvic fluid — then recommend US or CT with IV contrast for definitive assessment if torsion is suspected.

Search pattern

  1. Compare ovaries bilaterally on every pelvic CT — asymmetric enlargement is the primary trigger. Measure the larger ovary in three dimensions.
  2. Assess ovarian morphology — peripheral follicle displacement, stromal edema/heterogeneity, high-attenuation hemorrhage.
  3. Trace the adnexal pedicle medially and inferiorly from the enlarged ovary — look for the whirlpool/beaked soft-tissue structure of the twisted infundibulopelvic ligament.
  4. Uterine position — ipsilateral deviation of the uterine fundus supports the diagnosis.
  5. Look for a lead-point mass — fat density (dermoid), simple or complex cyst, cystadenoma.
  6. IV contrast enhancement — absent or diminished ovarian enhancement implies vascular compromise / infarction.
  7. Free pelvic fluid — trace to moderate; large hemoperitoneum widens the differential.
  8. Exclude mimics — check the appendix (appendicitis), the uterus (fibroids), and the adnexa for TOA features (thick-walled collection, bilateral involvement).
  9. Confirm βhCG status — exclude ectopic pregnancy; if positive, heterotopic pregnancy must be considered.
  10. Call it — don't hedge: if findings are suspicious, explicitly state "findings are concerning for ovarian torsion; emergent gyn consult recommended" and communicate directly.
