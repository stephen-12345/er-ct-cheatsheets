Ascending Cholangitis on CT

Biliary obstruction with retrograde bacterial infection of the bile ducts — identify the level, cause, and severity so the team can move to urgent drainage. Replace [bracketed] text in dictations.

🚨 Obstructed infected bile is a surgical/GI emergency. Suppurative (Grade III) cholangitis mandates urgent biliary drainage (ERCP or PTC) and ICU-level support. Call the team before you finish dictating.

💡PearlCT grades severity; MRCP defines anatomy

CT establishes biliary obstruction, identifies the cause (stone vs stricture vs mass), and detects life-threatening complications (hepatic abscesses, portal vein involvement). MRCP or ERCP then provides higher-resolution biliary roadmapping before drainage.

Primary CT Findings

Biliary duct dilatation

🔍How to find itExtrahepatic & intrahepatic ducts
  • Common bile duct (CBD): measure at widest point in the hepatoduodenal ligament on axial portal-phase. Normal ≤8 mm (≤10 mm post-cholecystectomy, ≤11 mm in elderly). Trace from the hilum to the ampulla.
  • Intrahepatic ducts (IHDs): normal 3rd-order branches are invisible. Dilatation >40% of adjacent portal vein caliber, or visible ducts paralleling portal veins ("parallel channel sign"), is abnormal.
  • Level of obstruction: distal CBD (stone, pancreatitis, ampullary lesion) vs mid CBD (stricture, cholangiocarcinoma) vs hilar (Klatskin tumour) vs diffuse (PSC, IgG4-related).
  • Abrupt vs gradual tapering: abrupt cutoff → stone or tumour; smooth fusiform tapering → benign stricture.
📏MeasureCBD diameter — always report
  • Measure outer-to-outer wall on axial image at widest point, in millimetres.
  • Correlate with patient age and prior cholecystectomy status.
  • Pneumobilia (gas in biliary tree) may decompress ducts — do not falsely reassure on caliber.

Duct wall thickening & enhancement

🔍How to find itConcentric wall changes — the inflammatory hallmark
  • Concentric duct-wall thickening (>1.5 mm) and hyperenhancement on portal-venous phase — the key CT sign distinguishing cholangitis from simple obstruction.
  • Best seen on the CBD in the hepatoduodenal ligament; look for similar changes in IHDs within the liver.
  • Periductal haziness/stranding of the hepatoduodenal ligament fat.

Periductal hepatic parenchymal enhancement

🔍How to find itTransient hepatic attenuation differences (THAD)
  • Wedge-shaped or segmental areas of early arterial-phase hyperenhancement adjacent to inflamed ducts, normalising on portal-venous phase.
  • Reflects hyperaemia and altered portal flow from periductal inflammation, not infarction.
  • Peripheral THAD in a hepatic segment + dilated IHDs in that segment → high specificity for ascending cholangitis.
  • Not present on all scans (depends on phase timing); absence does not exclude cholangitis.

Obstructing distal CBD stone

🔍How to find itTarget sign at the ampulla
  • Scroll the CBD from the confluence down to the ampulla in axial + coronal MPR. A stone appears as a hyperdense or isodense filling defect in a dilated duct, often with a target sign (hyperdense rim) or crescent sign (bile crescent around the stone).
  • Most choledocholithiasis stones are visible on unenhanced CT (hyperdense, 100–200 HU); pigment stones may be isodense — rely on portal-phase filling defect.
  • Measure distal CBD stone in mm (longest diameter); note distance from the ampulla.
  • Always look for cholelithiasis and gallbladder wall changes (concomitant acute cholecystitis in ~15%).
⚠️PitfallIsodense stones and soft-tissue masses can mimic each other
  • Cholesterol/soft stones may be isodense on contrast CT — a non-enhancing filling defect in a dilated CBD needs MRCP to characterise.
  • An ampullary or distal CBD tumour can appear identical to a stone on CT. Look for soft-tissue mass enhancement and lymphadenopathy.
  • Periampullary duodenal fold thickening can simulate a mass — evaluate on coronal/oblique MPR.

Complications to Call

Suppurative cholangitis & hepatic abscesses

🚨Critical findingSuppurative cholangitis — emergent drainage
  • Pus under pressure in the biliary tree. CT may show: gas within the biliary tree without prior instrumentation, extreme duct dilatation, periductal fat infiltration.
  • Hepatic microabscesses: multiple small (<1 cm) hypoenhancing round foci in a periductal distribution — pathognomonic when clustered along bile ducts. May coalesce into a large abscess.
  • Any hepatic abscess in the context of biliary obstruction = biliary source until proven otherwise. Measure and count dominant collections.
  • Portal vein involvement: periportal thickening, periportal oedema tracking along portal tracts (periportal tracking sign), and in severe cases pylephlebitis (portal vein thrombosis + gas).

Pneumobilia

🔍How to find itGas in the biliary tree — central distribution
  • Branching gas in the central/hilar biliary tree, following the biliary distribution — contrast with portal venous gas which is peripheral (<2 cm from liver capsule).
  • Causes: prior ERCP/sphincterotomy, biliary-enteric anastomosis, biliary stent, or gas-forming organisms (emphysematous cholangitis).
  • Pneumobilia from gas-forming infection without prior instrumentation = critical finding, emergent drainage and broad-spectrum antibiotics.
⚠️PitfallPneumobilia vs portal venous gas
  • Portal venous gas (PVG) is peripheral, tracks to within 2 cm of the liver capsule on the portal phase. Pneumobilia is central and hilar.
  • PVG in the right clinical context (bowel ischaemia, necrosis) is also a critical finding — do not dismiss it as pneumobilia.

Portal & vascular complications

🚨Critical findingPylephlebitis & portal vein thrombosis
  • Pylephlebitis: septic thrombophlebitis of the portal vein — hypoattenuating or gas-containing thrombus in the portal vein in the context of biliary/abdominal sepsis.
  • Periportal tracking on portal-phase CT: low-attenuation halo around portal triads reflecting oedema/inflammation propagating along portal tracts.
  • Hepatic artery: rarely involved but look for adjacent inflammatory changes if cholangitis is prolonged.

Protocol & Technique

CT protocol for suspected cholangitis

🧪ProtocolRecommended approach
  • Unenhanced phase: essential for detecting calcified CBD stones (hyperdense); identifying pneumobilia and portal venous gas without confusion from contrast.
  • Arterial phase (optional): best for THAD / periductal parenchymal hyperenhancement; also detects vascular complications.
  • Portal-venous phase: most useful overall — duct wall enhancement, filling defects in ducts, hepatic abscesses, pylephlebitis.
  • Thin sections + coronal/sagittal MPR: mandatory to trace the biliary tree fully, especially the distal CBD and ampullary region.
  • MRCP: superior to CT for non-calcified stones, stricture characterisation, and biliary anatomy planning before ERCP. Order when CT is inconclusive or ERCP is being planned.
💡PearlAlways trace the CBD to the ampulla on coronal MPR

The distal CBD can be obscured on axial images by duodenal gas or bowel. A dedicated thin-section coronal MPR through the hepatoduodenal ligament and into the pancreatic head is the single most useful reconstruction for identifying a distal CBD stone.

Search pattern

  1. CBD caliber: measure on axial portal-phase at widest point, then trace on coronal MPR from hilum to ampulla. Record the diameter and identify the level and cause of obstruction.
  2. Intrahepatic ducts: look for parallel-channel sign, note distribution (diffuse vs segmental — a segmental IHD dilatation may point to a peripheral stricture or tumour).
  3. Wall changes: concentric thickening and hyperenhancement of the CBD and IHDs — present in cholangitis, absent in simple obstruction.
  4. Periductal parenchyma: check arterial phase for THAD (wedge hyperenhancement adjacent to inflamed ducts).
  5. Obstructing lesion: stone (target sign, pre-contrast hyperdensity), stricture (smooth tapering, history), tumour (enhancing mass, double-duct sign, LN, vascular involvement).
  6. Complications: hepatic abscesses (rim-enhancing periductal foci), pneumobilia (central biliary gas), portal venous gas (peripheral), pylephlebitis (portal vein thrombus ± gas), concomitant cholecystitis.
  7. Severity clues: volume of pus under pressure is not directly visible on CT — the constellation of extreme duct dilatation + wall enhancement + abscesses + gas + patient instability = Grade III; alert the team immediately.
