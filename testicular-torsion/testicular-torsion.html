Testicular Torsion on Imaging

Scrotal ultrasound with color Doppler is the primary imaging modality. Clinical diagnosis must not be delayed — absent flow on a symptomatic side is a surgical emergency. Replace [bracketed] text.

🚨 Time-critical: Testicular salvage rate is >90% within 6 hours, ~50% at 12 hours, and <10% after 24 hours. Do not delay surgical exploration for imaging when clinical suspicion is high. If US is unavailable or equivocal, go straight to the OR.

Modality & Protocol

Imaging protocol

🧪ProtocolScrotal ultrasound with Doppler
  • High-frequency linear transducer (12–18 MHz) for optimal resolution.
  • Evaluate both testes side-by-side — the normal side is the essential control for Doppler comparison.
  • Grayscale B-mode: testicular size, echogenicity, homogeneity, epididymis, tunica vaginalis fluid, scrotal wall.
  • Color Doppler: intratesticular flow — use identical gain and PRF settings on both sides.
  • Spectral Doppler: confirm arterial waveform (diastolic flow) when color is equivocal.
  • High-sensitivity power Doppler / microvascular imaging if available — more sensitive for low-flow states.
  • Note the spermatic cord at the external inguinal ring — look for the whirlpool/spiral sign.
💡PearlCT has no primary role in torsion

CT is not the first-line test and cannot reliably demonstrate Doppler flow. It may incidentally show a high-riding, enlarged, or heterogeneous testis with ipsilateral spermatic cord thickening, but a normal CT does not exclude torsion. If the clinical scenario reveals torsion on CT or CT is being done for acute scrotum, call it and escalate immediately.

Diagnostic Findings

Doppler — the key finding

🔍FindingAbsent or reduced intratesticular Doppler flow
  • Absent intratesticular color Doppler flow on the symptomatic side vs. normal flow on the contralateral side — the most important sign of complete torsion.
  • Reduced flow (asymmetric, < contralateral side) indicates partial torsion or early/incomplete torsion — do not dismiss.
  • Reactive hyperemia of the peritesticular/epididymal tissues with absent intratesticular flow can occur — do not interpret peripheral flow as intratesticular.
  • In neonates and infants, intratesticular flow may be absent in normal small testes — clinical correlation and expert review essential.
📏MeasureTesticular size and volume

Measure testicular volume using the prolate ellipsoid formula: L × W × H × 0.523. Enlargement of the affected testis (>10–15% versus contralateral) supports the diagnosis. Volume asymmetry >20% is significant. The affected testis often appears globular rather than oval.

Grayscale findings

🔍FindingB-mode parenchymal changes
  • Early (<6 h): Testis may appear normal in echogenicity on grayscale — Doppler is the discriminator.
  • Subacute (6–24 h): Diffuse hypoechogenicity from edema and vascular congestion; testicular enlargement.
  • Late (>24 h / infarction): Heterogeneous echogenicity with focal hypoechoic regions of hemorrhagic infarct; may have hyperechoic foci (hemorrhage). Testis may be shrunken in chronic cases.
  • Reactive hydrocele: Anechoic peritesticular fluid is nearly universal.
  • Scrotal wall thickening / skin edema — secondary to vascular congestion.
  • Enlarged, hypoechoic epididymis due to venous congestion (not infection).

Whirlpool / torsion knot sign

🔍FindingSpermatic cord whirlpool sign
  • Scan the spermatic cord at and above the external inguinal ring — look for a twisted, rounded echogenic mass with a swirling/concentric appearance ("whirlpool" or "snail sign").
  • On color Doppler, the twisted cord shows a spiral color-flow pattern — often the most specific sign when visualized.
  • The torsion knot is typically located at the external inguinal ring or just superior to the testis.
  • Sensitivity of the whirlpool sign is ~89–100% in experienced hands; specificity ~99%.
  • Document the number of cord twists if visible (determines surgical approach).

Bell-clapper deformity & predisposing anatomy

💡PearlBell-clapper deformity
  • The bell-clapper deformity results from a high insertion of the tunica vaginalis onto the spermatic cord, allowing the testis to hang freely and rotate within the vaginal cavity like a clapper inside a bell.
  • Present in ~12% of males; bilateral in the majority — thus contralateral orchiopexy is performed simultaneously at surgery.
  • On ultrasound: a horizontal lie of the testis ("transverse lie") in the scrotum is a sonographic proxy for bell-clapper anatomy.
  • The most common age group is adolescent males (12–18 years), with a second peak in neonates.

Partial / intermittent torsion

⚠️PitfallReduced flow ≠ safe — partial torsion is surgical
  • Partial torsion (<360°) may preserve some Doppler flow — the testis may even appear normal or hyperemic if spontaneous detorsion has occurred.
  • Intermittent torsion: Episodic pain with normal ultrasound during asymptomatic periods. Horizontal testicular lie supports the diagnosis — orchiopexy is still indicated.
  • Any asymmetric reduction in flow in the clinical context of acute scrotal pain warrants surgical consultation, not watchful waiting.
  • Do not dismiss torsion because "there is some flow" — the sonographic findings must always be interpreted alongside the clinical presentation.

Differential Diagnosis

Torsion vs. epididymo-orchitis (EO)

🚨CriticalIncreased flow = epididymo-orchitis; absent flow = torsion — but do not anchor on this alone

The critical danger is mistaking torsion for epididymo-orchitis. EO typically shows markedly increased (hyperemic) intratesticular and epididymal flow. However, late torsion with reactive inflammation can mimic EO clinically. Use all signs together; when in doubt, treat as torsion.

Other differentials

⚠️PitfallOther causes of acute scrotum
  • Torsion of appendix testis / appendix epididymis: "Blue dot sign" clinically; US shows a small avascular nodule adjacent to the testis/epididymis with normal intratesticular flow — conservative management.
  • Incarcerated / strangulated inguinal hernia: Bowel in the inguinal canal or scrotum on US or CT.
  • Testicular trauma / hematocele: History of trauma; heterogeneous testis with peritesticular hematoma.
  • Testicular tumor: Mass lesion, may present with pain — biopsy/orchiectomy pathway, not exploration for torsion.
  • Henoch-Schönlein purpura: Scrotal involvement in young boys; systemic features.
  • Fournier gangrene: Scrotal gas on CT; systemic toxicity — surgical emergency distinct from torsion.

Late Presentation & Infarction

Testicular infarction

🔍FindingMissed / late torsion with infarction
  • After ~24–48 hours: global heterogeneity with mixed hypo- and hyperechoic regions; complete absence of Doppler flow.
  • Peripheral rim enhancement may be present at the tunica albuginea while the parenchyma is infarcted (viable shell around necrotic core).
  • The testis may be grossly enlarged or, in chronic missed cases, markedly atrophied.
  • Orchiectomy is likely at this point; however, the decision is surgical — do not state the testis is unsalvageable in the report unless you are certain.
  • Recommend direct surgical consultation regardless of imaging "salvageability."

Search pattern

  • 1. Set up both testes side-by-side — identical gain and PRF on Doppler for both sides.
  • 2. Assess the spermatic cord at the external inguinal ring — look for the whirlpool / spiral twist sign.
  • 3. Color Doppler intratesticular flow — compare sides; note absent, reduced, or increased flow.
  • 4. Spectral Doppler — confirm arterial waveform bilaterally; document asymmetry.
  • 5. Grayscale — testicular size, echogenicity (homogeneous vs. heterogeneous), epididymis, hydrocele, scrotal wall.
  • 6. If absent flow + clinical symptoms → call surgical team immediately; do not wait for a report.
  • 7. If increased flow → favor epididymo-orchitis; still document cord, exclude torsion of appendix testis.
  • 8. Assess testicular lie — horizontal ("transverse lie") supports bell-clapper deformity.
