Search pattern

  1. Survey sagittal T2: scan all lumbar levels for disc morphology, CSF effacement, marrow signal, and any epidural collection or mass. Identify the conus tip and its level.
  2. Identify suspect levels: note every level with thecal sac encroachment; rank by severity. Focus on L4–L5 and L5–S1 for disc pathology.
  3. Measure canal compromise: on axial T2 at each affected level — estimate cross-sectional thecal sac area and AP diameter. Note nerve root crowding or displacement.
  4. Characterize the cause: disc extrusion vs stenosis vs collection vs mass. Use CT bone windows (if available) for fracture detail; gadolinium for abscess/tumor.
  5. Look for associated findings: discitis/osteomyelitis (marrow T2/STIR changes, end-plate destruction); posterior ligamentous complex injury (trauma); skip metastases on full spine.
  6. Assess the conus: confirm no conus signal change (myelopathy vs cauda equina — different prognosis and management).
  7. Communicate and document: any finding consistent with CES requires immediate direct verbal communication with the treating team. Record time, recipient, and method.

Clinical Context & Red Flags

Red-flag clinical features

Cauda equina syndrome (CES) arises from compression of the lumbar nerve roots below the conus medullaris (typically below L1–L2). Classic presenting features that mandate urgent imaging:

  • Saddle anesthesia — numbness/decreased sensation in perineum, buttocks, inner thighs (S3–S5 distribution)
  • Bilateral sciatica — radiculopathy in both legs simultaneously
  • Urinary retention or overflow incontinence — the most sensitive neurological sign; painless retention in the context of back pain is CES until proven otherwise
  • Reduced anal sphincter tone and fecal incontinence
  • Lower extremity motor weakness — may be asymmetric early; progressive
  • Sexual dysfunction — erectile/ejaculatory dysfunction, loss of vaginal sensation
🚨CriticalIncomplete vs complete CES

CES incomplete (CESI): altered bladder/bowel function but not total retention — still urgent, surgical outcome better than complete.

CES with retention (CESR): painless urinary retention — emergency decompression required; delays beyond 24–48 hours significantly worsen prognosis for continence recovery.

Imaging Approach

Modality selection

🧪ProtocolMRI lumbar spine — first-line
  • Sagittal T2 — survey entire lumbar spine; identify level, disc morphology, CSF effacement, cord signal change
  • Axial T2 through each suspect level — assess canal cross-sectional area, nerve root crowding, thecal sac compression
  • Sagittal T1 — marrow signal, identify fat/blood; useful for metastases and epidural hematoma
  • Sagittal STIR — marrow edema, discitis/osteomyelitis, acute fracture
  • Gadolinium + fat-sat sequences if abscess, metastases, or leptomeningeal disease suspected
🧪ProtocolCT and CT myelogram — when MRI is contraindicated
  • Non-contrast CT lumbar spine: best for bony anatomy — burst fractures, retropulsed fragments, spondylosis, calcified disc; limited soft-tissue contrast
  • CT myelogram: intrathecal iodinated contrast (fluoroscopic injection) then immediate CT; outlines thecal sac contour and nerve roots; near-MRI sensitivity for disc herniation and canal stenosis; used for pacemakers, spinal cord stimulators, ferromagnetic implants
  • CT with IV contrast: epidural abscess, hematoma, metastatic disease — less specific than MRI but rapidly available
💡PearlDo not delay for MRI if patient is deteriorating

If MRI is not immediately available (> 1–2 h delay) and clinical CES is strongly suspected, proceed to CT myelogram rather than wait. In a trauma setting, plain CT of the lumbar spine will identify retropulsed fragments and is the fastest first step.

Key Imaging Findings

Large central disc herniation

🔍How to find itCentral/paracentral disc causing CES
  • Level: most commonly L4–L5 and L5–S1; always check L3–L4
  • Disc morphology: large central extrusion or sequestrated fragment; low T2 signal (desiccated disc); may contain gas (vacuum phenomenon on CT)
  • Thecal sac: effacement or obliteration of anterior CSF space; absent posterior CSF mantle at the affected level
  • Nerve roots: crowded, displaced posteriorly and laterally; loss of individual root silhouettes in the thecal sac
  • Axial T2: disc material fills > 50% of the canal cross-sectional area in severe cases
📏MeasurementCanal compromise thresholds
  • AP canal diameter: normal ≥ 15 mm at L4–L5; absolute stenosis < 10 mm; relative stenosis 10–13 mm
  • Cross-sectional area of thecal sac (axial T2): < 75 mm² severe stenosis; < 40 mm² extreme/critical stenosis with CES risk
  • Disc herniation occupying > 1/3 of canal area warrants explicit comment; > 1/2 is high risk for CES
  • Trefoil canal deformity (congenitally narrow, triangular canal) markedly increases risk at smaller disc volumes — always note the baseline canal shape

Severe canal stenosis

🔍How to find itDegenerative stenosis causing CES
  • Multilevel spondylotic change: disc height loss, end-plate osteophytes, ligamentum flavum hypertrophy (> 4–5 mm), and facet joint hypertrophy/effusion
  • Trefoil canal — symmetric posterior indentation from hypertrophied ligamentum flavum bilaterally; severe when CSF space completely effaced
  • Acquired spondylolisthesis (usually L4–L5) adds dynamic anteroposterior compression; correlate with upright/flexion-extension imaging if available
  • On MRI T2 sagittal: confluent dark signal (loss of CSF high signal) through multiple contiguous levels = "closed canal"; look for nerve root redundancy (tortuous, piled-up roots above the stenosis)

Epidural hematoma and abscess

🔍How to find itEpidural collection
  • Epidural hematoma: spontaneous (anticoagulation, AVM, coagulopathy) or post-procedural; biconvex or spindle-shaped collection dorsal to dura; hyperacute = isointense T1/heterogeneous T2; subacute = T1 bright; CT = hyperdense; may extend multiple levels
  • Epidural abscess: rim-enhancing fluid in epidural space; associated discitis (low T1, high T2 disc with end-plate destruction) and vertebral osteomyelitis; restricted diffusion (DWI); fever + elevated CRP/ESR; IV drug use, recent procedure, immunosuppression are risk factors
  • Both: mass effect on thecal sac with nerve root displacement; measure craniocaudal extent and degree of thecal sac compression

Metastatic epidural spinal cord compression (MESCC)

🔍How to find itMetastatic compression
  • Pedicle erosion — the "winking owl" sign on AP radiograph/axial CT; pedicle loss at one or more levels is a red flag
  • Vertebral body replacement by soft-tissue mass (low T1, variable T2, avid enhancement); pathological compression fracture (posterior convexity of fractured body, posterior element involvement, epidural component)
  • Epidural soft-tissue component extending posteriorly to displace or deform the thecal sac; grade the compression: 0 (no epidural disease) → 1a/b/c (epidural, no cord contact) → 2 (cord contact, CSF visible) → 3 (cord contact, no CSF visible) — Bilsky MESCC scale
  • MRI whole spine or at minimum full lumbar spine; metastases may be multilevel
  • CT: bony destruction best characterized; bone windows essential
💡PearlDistinguishing pathological from osteoporotic fracture

Features favoring metastatic/pathological fracture: posterior vertebral body convexity (versus concavity in osteoporotic), involvement of posterior elements (pedicle), epidural soft-tissue mass, other spinal lesions, known primary malignancy, absent acute marrow edema in adjacent vertebra. STIR is the most sensitive sequence for marrow replacement.

Traumatic canal compromise

🔍How to find itBurst fracture with retropulsion
  • CT bone windows — axial and sagittal reconstructions; look for retropulsed posterior cortical fragment into the spinal canal
  • Measure percent canal compromise on axial CT: (normal canal area − residual canal area) / normal canal area × 100
  • Comminuted vertebral body, loss of posterior vertebral body line, widened interpediculate distance (burst pattern)
  • Posterior element fractures increase instability; check for facet dislocation
  • MRI adds: disc herniation at fracture level, epidural hematoma, cord/conus signal change, posterior ligamentous complex integrity
⚠️PitfallDon't miss the conus level

In lower thoracic (T12) and thoracolumbar junction (L1) burst fractures, the conus medullaris itself may be compressed — this is a spinal cord injury, not cauda equina, and has different prognostic implications. Always identify conus tip level (normally between T12 and L2) and assess for conus signal change on T2/STIR.

Pitfalls & Pearls

⚠️PitfallCT cannot reliably exclude CES

A plain CT may show disc herniation but severely underestimates thecal sac compression compared to MRI. CT without myelography should not be used to exclude surgically significant canal compromise. If CT is negative or equivocal and clinical suspicion remains, MRI or CT myelogram is mandatory.

⚠️PitfallMultilevel disease — find the critical level

Degenerative spondylosis is often multilevel. The most stenotic level may not be the symptomatic one. Correlate with dermatomal distribution, unilateral vs bilateral symptoms, and if the clinical picture is unclear, document all levels with thecal sac cross-sectional area measurements to guide surgical planning.

💡PearlRedundant nerve roots — a sign of chronic severe stenosis

On sagittal MRI, tortuous ("piled up") nerve roots seen cranial to a high-grade stenosis indicate chronic severe canal narrowing. These may be mistaken for an intradural tumor (rare). Redundant roots are associated with neurological deficits and confirm functional significance of the stenosis even if symptoms are of gradual onset.

💡PearlCommunicate directly, document communication

CES is a time-critical diagnosis with outcomes directly tied to decompression timing. Always call the referring clinician or spinal surgery team directly for any finding consistent with CES. Document the name of the person contacted, the time, and the method of communication in the report. This is a medicolegal imperative.

🚨CriticalDo not hold for formal report

Findings consistent with CES should be communicated immediately by telephone to the treating team before the final report is signed. A preliminary verbal report with the key finding and level is sufficient to trigger urgent surgical evaluation. Document time and recipient of communication.
