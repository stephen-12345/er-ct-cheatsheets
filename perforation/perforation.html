🚨 Pneumoperitoneum = perforation until proven otherwise. Use lung windows to detect small locules. Then localize: the cluster of gas, focal wall defect, and adjacent inflammation point to the source.

Detecting Free Air

Where free gas collects

🔍How to find itUse lung windows and the non-dependent spots
  • Free gas rises to non-dependent locations supine: anterior to the liver, falciform ligament, perihepatic, and in the central mesentery.
  • Signs: Rigler sign (gas on both sides of the wall), falciform ligament sign, football sign (large volume).
  • Always scroll on lung/wide windows — tiny locules are invisible on soft-tissue windows.
⚠️PitfallFree air that isn't from perforation
  • Recent surgery / laparoscopy / paracentesis (can persist days), PD catheter, mechanical ventilation tracking from chest, transdiaphragmatic from pneumothorax/pneumomediastinum.
  • Pelvic gas in women may enter via the genital tract.
  • Correlate with history before calling a surgical perforation.

Localizing the Leak

Pinpoint the source

💡PearlFollow the gas + fluid to the wall defect
  • Focal bowel-wall discontinuity with concentrated extraluminal gas/fluid adjacent = the site.
  • Upper GI (gastroduodenal): gas in the lesser sac / perihepatic; ulcer history; periduodenal fluid.
  • Distal small bowel / colon: gas tracks along mesentery; feculent fluid; large-volume free air more typical of colon.
  • Extravasated oral/rectal contrast confirms and localizes the site.
📏NoteRetroperitoneal vs intraperitoneal

Retroperitoneal gas (around the duodenum, ascending/descending colon, rectum) localizes the leak to a retroperitoneal segment — e.g., second-part duodenal or extraperitoneal rectal perforation — and tracks into the pararenal spaces / psoas rather than the peritoneal cavity.

Perforated gastroduodenal ulcer

F: Moderate-volume pneumoperitoneum, most pronounced in the perihepatic region and lesser sac, with locules of gas and fluid adjacent to the [duodenal bulb/antrum] and a focal wall defect. Periduodenal fat stranding. [Extravasated oral contrast.]

I: Pneumoperitoneum with findings localizing to a perforated [gastroduodenal] ulcer. Surgical emergency — communicated to [name] at [time].

Colonic perforation

F: Large-volume pneumoperitoneum with extraluminal gas and fluid adjacent to the [sigmoid] colon and a focal wall defect, with surrounding fat stranding [and a pericolic collection]. [Underlying diverticular disease / obstructing mass.]

I: [Sigmoid] colonic perforation with pneumoperitoneum and peritonitis. Communicated to [name] at [time].

Tiny pneumoperitoneum, source uncertain

F: A few locules of free intraperitoneal gas anterior to the liver, identified on lung windows, without a definite wall defect, focal fluid collection, or extraluminal contrast. No prior surgery by available history.

I: Small pneumoperitoneum of uncertain source; perforation not excluded. Recommend clinical correlation [± surgical consultation; consider water-soluble contrast study].

Search pattern

  • 1. Scroll lung windows for free gas in non-dependent locations.
  • 2. Estimate volume; intraperitoneal vs retroperitoneal.
  • 3. Find the wall defect / gas-fluid cluster / extravasated contrast to localize.
  • 4. Identify the cause (ulcer, diverticulitis, tumor, obstruction, ischemia, trauma).
  • 5. Exclude benign causes (recent surgery/procedures); communicate.
