Acute Appendicitis on CT

Find the appendix, size it, grade it, and call perforation. Copy-ready findings/impressions — replace [bracketed] text.

⚠️ The surgical questions are: is it perforated (extraluminal air/fluid, abscess, focal wall defect), is there an appendicolith, and is it an appendiceal/cecal mass or mucocele rather than simple appendicitis.

Finding the Appendix

How to find it

🔍How to find itFollow the cecum to the base
  • Identify the cecum, then trace to the ileocecal valve; the appendix arises from the posteromedial cecum ~2.5 cm below the valve, where the taeniae coli converge.
  • Scroll caudally and trace the tubular blind-ending structure. Watch for retrocecal, pelvic, and subhepatic positions.
  • A normal appendix is ≤ 6 mm, often contains air/contrast, with a thin non-enhancing wall and clean periappendiceal fat.

Diagnostic Findings

Primary & secondary signs

📏ThresholdDiameter

Appendiceal diameter > 6 mm (outer wall to outer wall) is the key threshold, but use it with secondary signs — a distended-but-collapsible tip without inflammation may be normal.

🔍SignsThe picture of acute appendicitis
  • Dilated (> 6 mm), fluid-filled, non-compressible appendix with wall thickening and mural hyperenhancement.
  • Periappendiceal fat stranding and free fluid.
  • Appendicolith (calcified, increases perforation risk).
  • Adjacent cecal apical thickening (cecal bar / arrowhead sign pointing to the obstructed orifice).
🚨PerforationSigns that change management
  • Extraluminal gas (focal locules near the appendix), focal wall defect.
  • Periappendiceal/pericecal abscess or phlegmon.
  • Extraluminal appendicolith; ileus; significant free fluid.
⚠️PitfallMimics & don't-miss alternatives
  • Appendiceal mucocele / neoplasm: cystic dilatation, mural calcification, ± wall nodularity — beware rupture (pseudomyxoma). Mention if appendix > ~1.5 cm and cystic.
  • RLQ mimics: mesenteric adenitis, terminal ileitis (Crohn), cecal diverticulitis, epiploic appendagitis, ovarian/tubal pathology, ureteric stone, typhlitis.
  • Stump appendicitis in the post-appendectomy patient.

Uncomplicated acute appendicitis

F: Dilated, fluid-filled appendix measuring [X] mm in maximal diameter with wall thickening and mural hyperenhancement, located in a [retrocecal] position. Periappendiceal fat stranding and trace free fluid. [Appendicolith at the base.] No extraluminal gas, abscess, or focal wall defect.

I: Acute uncomplicated appendicitis. No CT evidence of perforation or abscess.

Perforated appendicitis with abscess

F: Dilated appendix ([X] mm) with a focal wall defect and adjacent extraluminal gas. [X × Y] cm rim-enhancing fluid collection in the [right iliac fossa / pelvis] consistent with abscess. Extensive periappendiceal stranding and free fluid. [Extraluminal appendicolith.]

I: Perforated appendicitis with a [X] cm periappendiceal abscess. [Consider percutaneous drainage.] Communicated to [name] at [time].

Negative for appendicitis

F: Normal appendix measuring [X] mm with a thin wall, intraluminal gas, and clean periappendiceal fat. No appendicolith, fat stranding, free fluid, or fluid collection.

I: Normal appendix; no CT evidence of appendicitis. [Alternative finding: …]

Search pattern

  • 1. Find the cecal base → trace the entire appendix to its tip.
  • 2. Diameter, wall thickening, enhancement, intraluminal contents (appendicolith).
  • 3. Periappendiceal fat stranding, free fluid, abscess, extraluminal gas.
  • 4. Cecal apex, terminal ileum, adnexa, ureters — alternative diagnoses.
  • 5. If perforated/abscess → state it and communicate.
