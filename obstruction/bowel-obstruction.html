Bowel Obstruction on CT

Find the transition point, name the cause, and call closed-loop / ischemia. Replace [bracketed] text.

🚨 The two phrases the surgeon needs: "closed-loop obstruction" and "signs of ischemia." Both are emergencies. Always describe the transition point and the cause.

Simple adhesive SBO

F: Dilated small-bowel loops up to [X] cm with collapsed distal small bowel and a transition point in the [pelvis] without an underlying mass, consistent with adhesive obstruction. Small-bowel feces sign proximal to the transition. Normal bowel-wall enhancement; no closed loop, pneumatosis, or free air.

I: Small-bowel obstruction with a [pelvic] transition point, most consistent with adhesions. No CT evidence of closed-loop obstruction or ischemia.

Closed-loop SBO with ischemia

F: Cluster of dilated, fluid-filled small-bowel loops in a [U/C] configuration converging on a single point with a mesenteric whirl. Decreased mural enhancement of the involved loops with mesenteric edema and free fluid. [Pneumatosis / portal venous gas.]

I: Closed-loop small-bowel obstruction with CT signs of bowel ischemia — surgical emergency. Communicated to [name] at [time].

Large-bowel obstruction from mass

F: Dilated colon to [X] cm with a transition at an annular shouldered [sigmoid] mass. Cecum measures [X] cm. [Competent ileocecal valve with closed-loop physiology.] No free air.

I: Large-bowel obstruction secondary to an obstructing [sigmoid] mass, suspicious for malignancy. [Cecal diameter approaching perforation threshold.]

Search pattern

  • 1. Dilated vs collapsed bowel — mechanical or ileus? SB or LB?
  • 2. Find and localize the transition point; name the cause.
  • 3. Check all hernia orifices.
  • 4. Closed loop? (converging loops, whirl). Ischemia? (enhancement, pneumatosis, PV gas).
  • 5. Cecal diameter for LBO; free air.

Establish the Pattern

SBO vs LBO vs ileus

📏CalibreThresholds
  • Small bowel dilated > 2.5–3 cm; large bowel > 6 cm (cecum > 9 cm = perforation risk).
  • Mechanical obstruction: dilated bowel proximally, collapsed bowel distally, with a transition point.
  • Ileus: diffusely dilated bowel without a transition point; gas to the rectum.
🔍How to find itWalk the bowel to the transition
  • Follow dilated loops to the point where calibre abruptly changes to collapsed bowel — that is the transition point; describe its location and cause.
  • Small-bowel feces sign (particulate material in dilated SB) marks the level just proximal to the transition.

Common causes by level

LevelUsual causes
SBOAdhesions (most common; transition with no mass), hernia, Crohn stricture, tumor, intussusception, gallstone ileus
LBOColorectal cancer (most common), volvulus (sigmoid/cecal), diverticular stricture, fecal impaction
💡PearlAlways check the hernia orifices

Inguinal, femoral, umbilical, incisional, internal, and obturator hernias are a common, fixable cause — a femoral or obturator hernia in an older woman is classic and easy to overlook.

The Emergencies

Closed-loop & ischemia

🚨Closed loopTwo points obstructed together
  • C- / U-shaped or radial dilated loop with the involved loops converging on a single point; mesenteric vessels converge / swirl at the torsion.
  • Whirl sign (twisted mesentery). High risk of strangulation.
🚨IschemiaStrangulation signs
  • Reduced / absent bowel-wall enhancement (most specific).
  • Wall thickening, mural / mesenteric edema, mesenteric fluid, congested vessels.
  • Pneumatosis and portal venous gas = late, ominous.
