Acute Ischemic Stroke on CT

NCCT early signs, ASPECTS scoring, hemorrhage exclusion, CTA for large-vessel occlusion, CT perfusion core/penumbra mismatch, and hemorrhagic transformation. Copy-ready findings/impressions — replace [bracketed] text.

🚨 Time is brain. IV thrombolysis window: 0–4.5 h from last known well (selected patients). Mechanical thrombectomy: up to 24 h with perfusion mismatch criteria. Identify LVO on CTA and communicate immediately — every minute of M1 occlusion costs ~1.9 million neurons. If NCCT shows no hemorrhage and ASPECTS ≥ 6, do not delay. Call the stroke team before finishing the report.

Protocol & Quality

CT Protocol for Acute Stroke

🧪ProtocolStandard code-stroke CT acquisition
  • NCCT head (5 mm, no contrast): exclude hemorrhage, assess ASPECTS, look for early ischemic changes and hyperdense vessel sign.
  • CTA head & neck: arterial phase (bolus-track on aortic arch; include from aortic arch to vertex). Evaluate for LVO, thrombus extent (clot burden), collateral grade, and tandem lesions (cervical ICA stenosis/dissection).
  • CT perfusion (if thrombectomy candidacy in question, especially late window or wake-up stroke): whole-brain 4D perfusion covering the posterior fossa if basilar suspected. Generate CBF, CBV, MTT, and Tmax maps. Apply automated mismatch software (RAPIDai or equivalent). Target: ischemic core volume < 70 mL and mismatch ratio > 1.8 for late-window thrombectomy (DAWN/DEFUSE-3 criteria).
  • Read NCCT on a stroke window (W:80, L:35 or W:40, L:40) to accentuate subtle grey-white contrast loss.

NCCT Early Ischemic Signs

Hyperdense Vessel Signs

🔍FindingHyperdense MCA / dot sign
  • Hyperdense MCA sign: unilateral high attenuation of the M1 segment (> 43 HU, or > 1.4× the contralateral M1). Represents acute thrombus. Present in ~50% of M1 occlusions.
  • Dot sign: hyperdense focus in the Sylvian fissure corresponding to an M2/M3 branch occlusion.
  • Hyperdense basilar artery: dense basilar on midline sagittal reformation — do not miss; posterior circulation stroke has high early mortality and is often underrecognized on NCCT.
  • Confirm on thin MIP from the CTA source images; T-junction / ICA terminus thrombus appears as a hyperdense "T" at the top of the ICA.
⚠️PitfallPseudo-hyperdense MCA

Elevated hematocrit (polycythemia, dehydration) raises attenuation of all vessels bilaterally — always compare to the contralateral MCA. True hyperdense MCA is unilateral and focally dense.

Early Parenchymal Ischemic Signs

🔍FindingGrey-white differentiation loss and sulcal effacement
  • Insular ribbon sign: loss of the sharp grey-white interface along the insular cortex (most sensitive early NCCT sign in MCA territory; cytotoxic edema begins within minutes).
  • Basal ganglia obscuration: the lentiform nucleus, caudate head, and internal capsule become isodense — "disappearing basal ganglia."
  • Loss of grey-white differentiation in the cortical ribbon of the frontal, parietal, or temporal operculum.
  • Sulcal effacement: local swelling of infarcted cortex compresses adjacent sulci — look for asymmetric sulcal narrowing compared to the contralateral hemisphere.
  • All these signs reflect cytotoxic edema (intracellular water shift); they are present within 1–3 h of infarct onset in large-territory strokes.
📏ThresholdAttenuation values
  • Normal grey matter: ~35–42 HU; normal white matter: ~28–34 HU. Grey-white ratio drops toward 1.0 in acute infarct.
  • Infarcted cortex typically < 32 HU within 6 h; < 28 HU suggests established infarct.
  • A whole-hemisphere hypodensity (malignant MCA syndrome) predicts massive edema and herniation — communicate urgently regardless of treatment eligibility.

📏ThresholdASPECTS cutoffs
  • ASPECTS 8–10: minimal early change; favorable prognosis for recanalization; do not withhold thrombolysis/thrombectomy on ASPECTS alone.
  • ASPECTS 6–7: moderate early change; thrombectomy still beneficial (HERMES meta-analysis).
  • ASPECTS ≤ 5: extensive infarct; higher risk of hemorrhagic transformation and futile recanalization; discuss with stroke team but do not unilaterally exclude.
  • ASPECTS 0–2: established large-territory infarct; thrombectomy unlikely to benefit; heightened hemorrhage risk with thrombolysis.

CT Perfusion — Core vs Penumbra

CT Perfusion Maps & Mismatch

🔍FindingInterpreting perfusion maps
  • CBF (cerebral blood flow): severely reduced in core (dead tissue, < 30% of normal). Irreversible injury.
  • CBV (cerebral blood volume): markedly reduced in core (autoregulation exhausted); preserved or elevated in penumbra (vasodilation compensating).
  • MTT / Tmax: prolonged (delayed) in both core and penumbra. Tmax > 6 s defines the at-risk penumbra in DAWN/DEFUSE-3 software.
  • Mismatch = Tmax > 6 s volume minus CBF-defined core volume. If mismatch ratio > 1.8 and absolute mismatch > 15 mL → salvageable penumbra, supports late-window thrombectomy (6–24 h).
  • Matched deficit (core ≈ Tmax lesion, no mismatch) = established infarct; thrombectomy less likely to provide benefit.
💡PearlLate-window and wake-up stroke selection

For patients presenting 6–24 h from last known well, or wake-up strokes with unknown onset, CT perfusion (or DWI-FLAIR mismatch on MRI) is mandatory to identify a salvageable penumbra. Automated software (RAPIDai) provides operator-independent core and penumbra volumes in minutes. Apply DAWN criteria (age/clinical/core mismatch) or DEFUSE-3 criteria (perfusion-imaging mismatch) before thrombectomy decision.

📏MeasureClot burden & collateral grade
  • Clot length: measure on MIP; longer clots (> 8 mm) less likely to recanalize with IV tPA alone → favors thrombectomy.
  • Collateral grade: compare opacification of leptomeningeal vessels in the ischemic territory vs the contralateral side. Good collaterals (≥ 50% filling) predict larger penumbra and better thrombectomy outcome.
  • Tandem occlusion: evaluate the cervical ICA (stenosis, dissection, plaque) — present in ~20% of anterior LVO; both sites must be treated.
  • Cervical ICA dissection: look for intimal flap, eccentric false lumen, tapered occlusion ("rat-tail"), or surrounding crescent high-T1 signal on MRI if available.
⚠️PitfallBasilar stroke — the great masquerader

Posterior circulation strokes are consistently underdiagnosed on NCCT and initial triage. The brainstem and cerebellum are prone to beam-hardening artifact. Always scroll through the posterior fossa on axial NCCT looking for hypodense cerebellar or brainstem signal, and generate a midline sagittal MIP on CTA to trace the entire basilar artery from the vertebrobasilar junction to the tip. A dense basilar on NCCT is pathognomonic but present in only ~70% of acute basilar occlusions. Missing basilar occlusion in a confused or comatose patient is a catastrophic oversight.

Hemorrhage Exclusion & Hemorrhagic Transformation

Hemorrhage Exclusion Before Thrombolysis

🚨CriticalNo IV tPA if hemorrhage is present

NCCT must demonstrate no intracranial hemorrhage before thrombolysis. On thin-section NCCT with appropriate windows:

  • Intracerebral hemorrhage: focal hyperdensity (> 50–60 HU) in parenchyma — distinguish from calcification (calcification is on prior imaging, bilateral in basal ganglia, sharply defined).
  • Subarachnoid hemorrhage: hyperdensity in the cisterns and sulci (less dense than parenchymal blood).
  • Subdural/epidural: extra-axial hyperdensity crescentic or biconvex.
  • Microbleeds: not visible on NCCT (require gradient echo/SWI MRI); their presence on prior MRI is a relative contraindication — check prior imaging if available.

Hemorrhagic Transformation

Occurs in up to 40% of large-territory infarcts, particularly after thrombolysis or thrombectomy. Classified by ECASS criteria:

Type NCCT Appearance Clinical Significance
HI1 (Hemorrhagic Infarction 1)Small petechiae along margins of infarctBenign; no clinical deterioration
HI2 (Hemorrhagic Infarction 2)Confluent petechiae within infarct, no mass effectBenign; often asymptomatic
PH1 (Parenchymal Hematoma 1)Hematoma ≤ 30% of infarct volume, mild mass effectMay be symptomatic; monitor
PH2 (Parenchymal Hematoma 2)Hematoma > 30% of infarct, significant mass effectSymptomatic; associated with neurological deterioration; communicate urgently
⚠️PitfallContrast staining vs early hemorrhagic transformation

After thrombectomy with contrast administration, contrast staining of infarcted tissue can mimic hemorrhage on immediate post-procedure NCCT — both appear hyperdense. Key distinction: contrast staining typically has HU 40–60 (slightly lower than blood), follows the expected infarct territory in a gyral pattern, and resolves or fades on a 24-hour follow-up NCCT as contrast washes out, whereas true hemorrhage persists or expands. If in doubt, repeat NCCT at 24 h or obtain a dual-energy CT to distinguish iodine from blood.

Search pattern

  • 1. NCCT — exclude hemorrhage first: adjust window (W:80, L:35); scan all lobes, posterior fossa, and extra-axial spaces.
  • 2. Look for hyperdense vessel: check M1 bilaterally (HU asymmetry), basilar on sagittal MIP, T-junction at ICA terminus.
  • 3. Early ischemic changes: insular ribbon, lentiform nucleus, grey-white cortical differentiation, sulcal effacement — narrow window (W:40, L:40) to accentuate contrast.
  • 4. ASPECTS score: mentally check all 10 regions at ganglionic and supraganglionic levels; document which are affected.
  • 5. CTA — trace arteries from aortic arch to vertex: ICA (cervical and intracranial), M1, M2, ACA, PCA, basilar, vertebrals. Confirm or exclude LVO; assess collaterals; measure clot length.
  • 6. CT perfusion (if obtained): review automated maps — identify ischemic core (CBF), penumbra (Tmax > 6 s), calculate mismatch ratio. Apply DAWN/DEFUSE-3 criteria if late window.
  • 7. Alternative diagnoses: scan for tumor, abscess, PRES, SDH, metabolic changes — do not anchor on stroke if the distribution is atypical.
  • 8. Communicate: positive findings (LVO, hemorrhage, PH2 transformation, basilar occlusion, malignant MCA) must be communicated immediately — document name and time.
