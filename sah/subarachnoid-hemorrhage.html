Subarachnoid Hemorrhage on CT

Aneurysmal · perimesencephalic · traumatic · cortical convexity patterns. Copy-ready findings and impressions — replace [bracketed] text. A workstation aid, not a substitute for local protocol or clinical correlation.

🚨 Thunderclap headache with any cisternal hyperdensity = aneurysmal SAH until proven otherwise. Call the clinician immediately, activate neurosurgery/interventional neuroradiology, and proceed to CTA of the head and neck without waiting to finish the dictation. Sensitivity of CT drops below 90% within 6 hours and to ~50% at 5–7 days — LP or MRI-FLAIR is mandatory when CT is negative but clinical suspicion is high.

Search pattern

  1. Set window/level to W 100, L 60 ("subdural windows") first to maximise sensitivity for thin blood in the basal cisterns and sulci before reading at brain windows.
  2. Systematically inspect the basal cisterns on each axial slice: suprasellar, ambient, perimesencephalic, quadrigeminal plate, prepontine, and interpeduncular cisterns.
  3. Check the sylvian fissures bilaterally and the anterior interhemispheric fissure for asymmetric or focal hyperdensity.
  4. Evaluate the ventricles: temporal horn size (hydrocephalus), IVH (blood casts in dependent portions), aqueduct/fourth ventricle obstruction.
  5. Inspect the cortical sulci over the convexity for traumatic or cortical convexity SAH; correlate with the clinical history.
  6. If blood is present, grade it with the modified Fisher scale and identify the dominant distribution to predict the likely aneurysm location.
  7. If CTA is performed, scrutinise the Circle of Willis vessels on thin-slab MIP and VR; for each aneurysm, measure sac diameter and neck width; identify the culprit by matching blood distribution.
  8. If the CT is negative but suspicion is high, explicitly state the limitation in the impression and recommend LP (≥12 h) or MRI-FLAIR/SWI.
  9. For any confirmed SAH: report modified Fisher grade, hydrocephalus, IVH, and communicate immediately before completing the dictation.

Orientation & Protocol

CT protocol and scan technique

🧪ProtocolGet the technique right — thin slices, bone algorithm, CTA
  • Non-contrast CT of the head (NCCT) first: 0.6–1 mm reconstruction, standard and bone-plus kernels. Axial + coronal reformats through the basal cisterns.
  • Blood is bright on NCCT (50–80 HU) against the normally low-density CSF. Sensitivity is ~98% within 6 h of ictus, ~90% at 24 h, falls sharply thereafter.
  • If NCCT is positive (or high clinical suspicion despite negative NCCT), perform CT angiography of the head and neck (vertex to aortic arch, 0.6 mm collimation, arterial phase) to identify the culprit aneurysm.
  • Thin-slice coronal and sagittal reformats from the CTA are essential for aneurysm neck characterization and coiling/clipping planning.
  • Negative CT + high suspicion: lumbar puncture for xanthochromia (≥12 h after headache onset) or MRI FLAIR/SWI before discharge.
  • Avoid contrast before NCCT — it can spuriously fill the cisterns and mimic pseudo-SAH.

NCCT detection — reading the cisterns

🔍How to find itHyperdense blood in cisterns and sulci

Acutely, blood is hyperdense (50–80 HU) relative to brain and CSF. Look in these locations systematically on every slice:

  • Basal cisterns: suprasellar cistern, ambient cisterns (between midbrain and temporal lobes), quadrigeminal plate cistern, perimesencephalic cisterns.
  • Sylvian fissures: blood filling the fissure bilaterally or asymmetrically — MCA territory.
  • Interhemispheric fissure: linear hyperdensity along the falx, especially anteriorly — ACOM territory.
  • Sulcal blood over the convexity: traumatic, RCVS, or CAA — distinguish from dural/subdural.
  • Fourth ventricle / aqueduct: intraventricular extension indicates severe bleed or basal source.
  • Look at the window/level: W 100, L 60 (subdural windows) to bring out subtle thin cisternal blood.

SAH Patterns & Etiology

Aneurysmal SAH — the don't-miss

🚨CriticalAneurysmal pattern — call the team now

Thick, diffuse cisternal blood filling the basal cisterns, typically centered on the Circle of Willis. Blood distribution helps predict the culprit vessel before CTA:

  • Interhemispheric fissure (anterior) + suprasellar → anterior communicating artery (ACOM) — most common site (~30–35%).
  • Sylvian fissure (unilateral or dominant) → MCA bifurcation (~20–25%).
  • Posterior fossa (prepontine + interpeduncular cistern) + IVH → basilar tip or PCOM.
  • Perimesencephalic / ambient cisterns + suprasellar with no focal sylvian or interhemispheric predominance → consider perimesencephalic (benign) pattern first, but rule out basilar aneurysm with CTA.
  • Posterior fossa / fourth-ventricular blood ± prepontine cistern → vertebral or PICA aneurysm.
💡PearlDistribution predicts the aneurysm — know it before CTA

Before scrolling through the CTA, note the dominant blood location. An ACOM aneurysm bleeds anteriorly and medially; MCA bleeds laterally into the sylvian fissure; basilar tip bleeds into the interpeduncular and perimesencephalic cisterns with IVH. This directs your attention on the CTA and reduces the chance of calling the wrong aneurysm when multiple are present.

CTA — finding and characterising the aneurysm

🔍How to find itScrutinise the Circle of Willis on thin MIP and 3D VR
  • Review thin axial source images (0.6 mm), thin-slab MIP (3–5 mm slabs in multiple planes), and 3D volume rendering for aneurysm detection and morphology.
  • Measure the maximum aneurysm sac diameter and neck width. Sac-to-neck ratio and dome-to-neck ratio matter for coiling candidacy.
  • Note aneurysm orientation (direction of fundus projection) — directs interventional approach and rupture risk.
  • When multiple aneurysms are present, the culprit is usually the one adjacent to the densest blood, or the largest, or the one with an irregular "daughter sac" / nipple.
  • Look for the focal hyperdensity adjacent to the aneurysm fundus on NCCT — the "spot" of sentinel bleed — as a surrogate for the ruptured dome.
📏MeasureAneurysm size and reporting essentials
  • Sac diameter: measure the largest single dimension on MIP/source; report in mm.
  • Neck width: relevant for deciding coiling vs clipping. Wide neck ≥4 mm or dome-to-neck ratio <1.5 may require stent-assisted coiling.
  • Daughter sac / lobulation / irregular surface: associated with rupture risk even for incidentally found aneurysms.
  • For planning: describe the aneurysm parent vessel, branch relationship, nearby perforators, and whether there is a bone/skull base relationship relevant for clip access.

Perimesencephalic SAH — the benign pattern

💡PearlPerimesencephalic pattern — good prognosis, but still needs CTA

About 10–15% of non-traumatic SAH follows a perimesencephalic (PM) / prepontine pattern, which is believed to arise from a venous or capillary source rather than aneurysm rupture:

  • Blood is confined to the cisterns immediately anterior to the midbrain: prepontine, perimesencephalic (ambient), and interpeduncular cisterns, not extending into the interhemispheric fissure or sylvian fissures.
  • The center of the bleed is anterior to the brainstem, not within the basal ganglia or ventricles.
  • If any blood is seen in the anterior interhemispheric fissure, sylvian fissure, or there is IVH, do not call it perimesencephalic — go directly to CTA.
  • Always perform CTA — 5–8% of PM-pattern SAHs have a basilar, PCOM, or vertebrobasilar aneurysm as the true cause. A negative CTA in a pure PM pattern is reassuring; DSA is not always required at some centres (discuss with neurosurgery).
  • Prognosis: low rebleed risk, low vasospasm, nearly no disability if truly PM. Hunt & Hess grade typically I–II.

Traumatic SAH

🔍How to find itConvexity sulcal blood, often over a contusion

Traumatic SAH (tSAH) is the most common CT finding in significant head injury. It differs from aneurysmal SAH in distribution and context:

  • Blood in cortical sulci over the convexity, especially at sites of impact (frontal, temporal, parietal poles).
  • Typically superficial, thin, and asymmetric; often adjacent to a cerebral contusion or subdural hematoma.
  • Not centered on the basal cisterns or Circle of Willis unless there is concurrent aneurysmal rupture from trauma (rare).
  • Amount and distribution predict outcome in TBI — use Marshall or Rotterdam CT scores for prognostication.
  • Even small amounts of tSAH visible in the Sylvian fissure or basal cisterns from trauma can indicate more severe injury (shearing, contusion base).

Cortical convexity SAH — the differential list

⚠️PitfallCortical sulcal blood without trauma — don't anchor on one diagnosis

Cortical convexity SAH (cSAH) is blood confined to one or a few sulci over the brain surface, remote from the basal cisterns. The differential is broad and depends on age:

  • RCVS (Reversible Cerebral Vasoconstriction Syndrome): young to middle-aged, often female; thunderclap headache; recurrent; CTA may show multifocal vasospasm of medium vessels ("beading"). Most common non-traumatic cause of cSAH in those <60 years.
  • CAA (Cerebral Amyloid Angiopathy): elderly; often posterior convexities (parieto-occipital); look for cortical microbleeds and superficial siderosis on MRI SWI — multiple "blooming" foci.
  • Dural venous sinus thrombosis (DVST): check for hyperdense sinus, gyral edema, venous infarct. Cortical venous thrombosis can cause sulcal blood.
  • Infective endocarditis / mycotic aneurysm: peripheral cortical location, septic emboli elsewhere.
  • Other: coagulopathy, moyamoya, dural AVM, vasculitis.
  • Always describe the distribution precisely (convexity vs basal vs mixed) and suggest MRI + MRV if cSAH is suspected on clinical grounds.

Complications

Acute hydrocephalus

🔍How to find itObstructive hydrocephalus from blood in the ventricles or cisterns

Occurs in 20–30% of aneurysmal SAH at presentation, due to blood obstructing CSF flow at the foramina of Luschka/Magendie, aqueduct, or by impaired CSF reabsorption (communicating hydrocephalus).

  • Look for enlargement of the temporal horns of the lateral ventricles (>2 mm) — the most sensitive early sign.
  • Dilatation of the third and fourth ventricles; periventricular hypodensity (transependymal CSF seepage = high ICP).
  • Intraventricular hemorrhage (IVH) is a major risk factor; blood casts in the fourth ventricle and aqueduct cause obstructive hydrocephalus.
  • Compare ventricular size to any prior imaging if available; even mild enlargement relative to baseline is significant.
  • Acute hydrocephalus is an indication for external ventricular drain (EVD) — call the team.

Rebleed

🚨CriticalRebleed — highest risk in the first 24 hours
  • Rebleed risk is highest in the first 24 hours (15–20%) and carries ~70% mortality. The absolute priority is to secure the aneurysm (coiling or clipping) as soon as possible.
  • On follow-up CT: new or expanding hemorrhage in the original cisterns, new IVH, or acute neurological deterioration should prompt immediate repeat imaging.
  • A new increase in SAH density or volume on the comparison scan = presumptive rebleed until proven otherwise.
  • Report the time of original CT and time of new CT so clinicians can correlate with the clinical course.

Vasospasm and delayed cerebral ischemia (DCI)

💡PearlDCI window: days 4–14 after ictus

Vasospasm is the narrowing of cerebral arteries from subarachnoid blood breakdown products; it causes DCI in up to 30% of survivors. CT angiography (or CT perfusion) is used to evaluate for vasospasm after day 4:

  • CTA: segmental or diffuse narrowing of the proximal intracranial arteries (A1–A2, M1–M2, P1, basilar, vertebral). Compare to the admission CTA.
  • CT perfusion (CTP): prolonged MTT/Tmax, reduced CBF, elevated CBV (vasospasm) or reduced CBV (infarct) maps. Used to guide intervention.
  • Severity of SAH blood on admission CT (modified Fisher 3–4) predicts highest DCI risk.
  • New infarct on follow-up CT in a vascular territory corresponding to a vasospastic vessel = DCI.
  • Communicate urgently if there is angiographic-grade vasospasm (>50% luminal narrowing) — patient may be candidate for intra-arterial therapy.

Intraventricular hemorrhage (IVH)

🔍How to find itBlood casts in the ventricles
  • Hyperdense blood layering in the occipital horns (most dependent on supine positioning), third, or fourth ventricle.
  • A complete blood cast of the fourth ventricle or aqueduct is an ominous sign indicating obstructive hydrocephalus.
  • Report the IVH volume and location; the Graeb score or LeRoux score can be used.
  • IVH correlates with higher modified Fisher grade and worse outcome; the team may need to escalate EVD to include intrathecal thrombolytics.

Pitfalls & Mimics

Pseudo-SAH and CT mimics

⚠️PitfallPseudo-SAH — diffuse cerebral edema, polycythaemia, contrast

Pseudo-SAH is the apparent hyperdensity of the basal cisterns in the absence of true hemorrhage, most often from severe diffuse cerebral oedema causing the brain to become isoattenuating or hypodense while normal meninges and vessels remain relatively hyperdense:

  • Causes: diffuse cerebral oedema (anoxic, malignant ICP), polycythaemia/haemoconcentration, leptomeningeal carcinomatosis, intrathecal contrast residual.
  • Distinguishing features: in pseudo-SAH, the hyperdensity is usually less focal, there is global cerebral hypodensity, no HU measurement above 50 HU on window/level analysis, and no clinical thunderclap headache history.
  • Measure HU in the suspected area; true SAH is typically 50–80 HU. If equivocal, perform LP.
  • Post-contrast CT can show enhancement of leptomeningeal tumour or inflammation mimicking SAH — do not read post-contrast CT for SAH unless a pre-contrast series exists for comparison.
⚠️PitfallNegative NCCT — LP or MRI-FLAIR before clearing the patient
  • CT sensitivity for SAH is ~98% at <6 h, ~90% at 24 h, ~80% at 3 days, and ~50% at 5–7 days. A negative CT does not exclude SAH.
  • All patients with a thunderclap headache and negative NCCT should have LP (≥12 h after onset) for xanthochromia or MRI with FLAIR/DWI/SWI.
  • FLAIR imaging detects SAH for 2–4 weeks; SWI detects superficial siderosis for months to years.
  • If LP is xanthochromic or MRI is positive, proceed to CTA/DSA even with a normal CT.
  • The word "no intracranial hemorrhage" in your report should never be used to discharge a thunderclap headache patient without LP or MRI follow-up — flag this in your impression.
⚠️PitfallDural versus subarachnoid — don't confuse the two
  • Subdural hematoma is crescentic, follows the inner table, crosses suture lines, and does not fill sulci. SAH fills sulci and cisterns and is bounded by the inner surface of the pia.
  • Thin subdural along the tentorium or falx can be mistaken for SAH. Use coronal reformats — subdural tracks along the tentorium surface; SAH follows the cisternal spaces below.
  • Hyperdense basilar artery or vein of Galen can simulate a prepontine cistern bleed — check axial and coronal MIP from CTA.
