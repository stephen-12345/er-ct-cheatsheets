Ectopic Pregnancy on Imaging

US is first-line — transvaginal ultrasound remains the primary modality. CT is often the incidental finder in the ED when a pregnancy is unsuspected or when the patient presents with undifferentiated abdominal pain. Know what CT can and cannot show, and always correlate with beta-hCG and TVUS. Replace [bracketed] text before signing.

🚨 Life-threatening emergency: In any patient of reproductive age with a positive beta-hCG, empty uterus on imaging, and free intraperitoneal fluid — assume ruptured ectopic pregnancy with hemoperitoneum until proven otherwise. Communicate immediately to the clinical team. This is a surgical emergency.

CT Findings

What CT shows in ectopic pregnancy

🧪ProtocolCT technique for suspected ectopic / pelvic emergency
  • Contrast-enhanced CT abdomen and pelvis (portal venous phase 65–70 s) is the typical ED exam. IV contrast is essential to characterize adnexal masses and assess for active bleeding.
  • No oral contrast is needed for an emergent pelvic evaluation — it delays imaging and rarely adds diagnostic value.
  • If the patient is hemodynamically unstable, do not delay resuscitation or surgical consultation for CT. A positive FAST exam with a positive beta-hCG is sufficient to go to the OR.
  • Post-contrast delayed phase (90–120 s) can help distinguish sentinel clot from active extravasation when rupture is suspected.
🔍Primary findingsCT signs of ectopic pregnancy
  • Empty uterus with no intrauterine gestational sac (Note: pseudogestational sac — see below).
  • Adnexal mass separate from the ovary: solid, cystic, or mixed; may show a thick echogenic ring on US ("tubal ring"). On CT, a ring-enhancing adnexal structure is highly suspicious.
  • Hemoperitoneum: high-attenuation free fluid (45–70 HU) in the cul-de-sac, paracolic gutters, perihepatic/perisplenic spaces in more advanced hemorrhage.
  • Sentinel clot sign: highest-attenuation blood adjacent to the rupture site — helps localize the source of hemorrhage.
  • Active contrast extravasation: blush of contrast pooling in a ruptured ectopic — surgical emergency.
  • Gestational sac with or without embryo: occasionally visible outside the uterus; yolk sac or fetal pole essentially confirms the diagnosis.
  • Ipsilateral corpus luteum cyst in the ovary (normal finding in early pregnancy — do not mistake for the ectopic).
📏Key measurementHemoperitoneum attenuation

Acute blood is 45–80 HU on CECT. The sentinel clot (highest-density blood at the bleeding site) is often 60–80 HU. Subacute/lysed blood may be lower attenuation (20–40 HU) and can be missed as simple fluid. Measure in a dependent location and compare to the bladder for calibration.

Interstitial (cornual) ectopic — high-risk variant

🚨CriticalInterstitial ectopic — highest rupture risk
  • Implanted in the intramural / interstitial segment of the tube within the cornual myometrium.
  • CT/MRI key feature: gestational sac is eccentric, in the lateral uterine wall; myometrial mantle < 5 mm around the sac — this is the surgical threshold.
  • Can grow larger before rupture (more vascular territory) → more catastrophic hemorrhage when it does rupture.
  • "Interstitial line sign" on US: echogenic line from the uterine cavity to the ectopic sac. Often missed; correlation with MRI or surgical exploration may be needed.
  • Rupture bleeds into the broad ligament or free peritoneal cavity — do not overlook a broad-ligament hematoma in an unstable patient.

Cesarean-scar ectopic

🚨CriticalCesarean-scar ectopic — uterine rupture risk
  • Implanted in or adjacent to the lower uterine segment cesarean-section scar. Most common in patients with prior cesarean delivery.
  • CT: gestational sac in the anterior lower uterine segment, often with thin or absent myometrium anteriorly, bulging toward the bladder.
  • Can invade the bladder (bladder-flap implantation) or bladder serosa — check for bladder wall irregularity.
  • Risk of massive hemorrhage and uterine rupture if untreated; requires specialist intervention (UAE, methotrexate, or surgical).

Cervical and ovarian ectopics

🔍Rare sitesCervical and ovarian ectopic
  • Cervical ectopic: gestational sac within the cervical canal, below the internal os. CT shows ballooning of the cervical canal with a contained sac. Profuse hemorrhage risk with instrumentation. Often treated with cervical injection or UAE.
  • Ovarian ectopic: sac within the ovarian parenchyma itself — criteria: tube on that side must be intact, sac must be surrounded by ovarian tissue. CT cannot reliably distinguish this from a corpus luteum. TVUS with Doppler ("ring of fire" in the ovary) and histology are needed.

Abdominal and heterotopic pregnancy

💡PearlAbdominal and heterotopic pregnancy
  • Abdominal ectopic: implanted anywhere in the peritoneal cavity — omentum, bowel serosa, mesentery, liver surface. Advanced abdominal pregnancy may present late with a fetus visible on CT in an unusual location. Always look for the uterus separately.
  • Heterotopic pregnancy: simultaneous intrauterine and ectopic pregnancy. Rare in natural conception (~1:30,000) but significantly more common with ART (assisted reproductive technology) (~1:100 to 1:500 in IVF). An IUP does NOT exclude an ectopic in an ART patient.
  • On CT: intrauterine gestational sac identified plus separate adnexal mass or free fluid — do not be reassured by the IUP alone in an ART patient with pain and free fluid.

Diagnostic Pitfalls

Pseudogestational sac vs true IUP

⚠️PitfallPseudogestational sac — do not call this an IUP
  • A pseudogestational sac is a small fluid collection (decidual reaction) within the uterine cavity in up to 20% of ectopic pregnancies — it can mimic an early IUP on CT or US.
  • Key distinguishing features on TVUS: a true gestational sac has a double decidual sac sign (two echogenic rings) and is eccentrically placed within the endometrium. A pseudosac is centrally located, surrounded by a single thin echogenic rim.
  • On CT: the distinction cannot be reliably made — CT cannot confirm an IUP. Always require TVUS correlation if the uterine contents are ambiguous.
  • The presence of a fluid-filled uterine structure on CT does not exclude ectopic pregnancy.
⚠️PitfallCommon misses and look-alikes
  • Corpus luteum cyst: ring-enhancing structure in the ovary — do not confuse with a tubal ectopic. CL cyst is within the ovary; a tubal ectopic is separate from the ovary.
  • Hemorrhagic ovarian cyst: high-attenuation cystic mass with fluid; may bleed into the pelvis — can exactly mimic ruptured ectopic. Beta-hCG is the key differentiator.
  • Small volume free fluid: physiologic follicular fluid is expected in the pelvis. High-attenuation pelvic fluid (> 30 HU) in a woman of reproductive age with pelvic pain demands beta-hCG and TVUS.
  • Dermoid / teratoma: well-defined adnexal mass with fat attenuation ± calcification — usually not confused with ectopic, but large ruptured dermoid causes chemical peritonitis with extensive pelvic fat stranding.
  • Appendicitis / right-sided adnexal torsion: RLQ pain mimics a right-sided ectopic — evaluate the appendix and right adnexa systematically.

💡Pearl"Discriminatory zone" — beta-hCG threshold

At beta-hCG > 1500–2000 mIU/mL (TVUS discriminatory zone), an intrauterine gestational sac should be visible on TVUS. Empty uterus above this threshold strongly suggests ectopic (or recent complete abortion). At lower levels, a viable IUP may be too early to see — serial beta-hCG and repeat TVUS are required. CT cannot substitute for this assessment.

Communication and management

🚨Critical communicationWhen to call immediately
  • Significant hemoperitoneum in a patient with positive beta-hCG and empty uterus — call the clinical team immediately. This is a surgical emergency.
  • Active extravasation on CECT from a pelvic mass — immediate surgical or IR consultation.
  • Interstitial or cesarean-scar ectopic identified on CT/MRI — call even if the patient is currently stable; these can rupture catastrophically without warning.
  • Document name, time, and contact method for critical communication in your report.

Search pattern

  • 1. Uterus first: Is there a normally positioned intrauterine gestational sac? If the uterus is empty — proceed with extreme suspicion regardless of the primary indication for CT.
  • 2. Adnexa bilaterally: Identify both ovaries and evaluate for any adnexal mass separate from the ovaries. Measure size, assess attenuation, look for ring enhancement.
  • 3. Lower uterine segment: In any patient with prior cesarean delivery — scrutinize the anterior LUS for an eccentrically implanted sac. Check the myometrial mantle thickness.
  • 4. Cornua: In each uterine cornu, assess for an eccentric sac. Measure myometrial mantle (< 5 mm = interstitial ectopic threshold).
  • 5. Cervix: Check the cervical canal for a contained sac below the internal os (cervical ectopic).
  • 6. Free fluid: Measure attenuation of pelvic free fluid. High attenuation (> 30–40 HU) = hemoperitoneum until proven otherwise. Scroll the entire abdomen — paracolic gutters, Morrison's pouch, perisplenic space.
  • 7. Sentinel clot: Find the highest-attenuation blood — localizes the site of hemorrhage and helps guide surgical or IR approach.
  • 8. Active extravasation: Compare arterial and portal phases for a contrast blush. Presence changes management to emergent intervention.
  • 9. In ART patients: Confirm IUP AND evaluate the adnexa independently — heterotopic pregnancy must be excluded.
  • 10. Communicate: Any suspected ectopic or hemoperitoneum in a pregnant patient requires immediate communication. Document in the report.
